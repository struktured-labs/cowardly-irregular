# The Assembly

## A Novella of World 4 — Industrial

---

### Prologue: Gray

There was no sky. That was the first thing. Not a clouded sky, not a dark sky, not a sky that was the wrong color. No sky at all. Just a ceiling — gray, flat, industrial — stretching in every direction, held up by pillars of riveted steel that vanished into a haze of fluorescent light.

The air tasted like recycled air. Not stale. Recycled. As though it had been used before, filtered, deemed acceptable, and sent back out for another cycle. It had the flavor of efficiency and the aftertaste of nothing.

The ground was concrete. Not cobblestone pretending to be something charming, not asphalt pretending to be practical. Concrete. Poured, cured, and forgotten by whoever poured it, because its purpose was to be stood on, not admired.

The transformation happened to them before they'd taken three steps.

In the steampunk world, the job evolutions had felt like upgrading. Better materials. More refined function. The Steamknight's wrench-sword had weight and purpose; the Steampriest's vestments had ceremony; the Alchemist's satchel had order; the Saboteur's coat had presence; the Busker's trumpet had resonance. There was craft to it. There was beauty in the precision, even when the precision was in service of control.

Here, there was no craft. There was just function.

The wrench-sword became a baton again — not the security guard's baton but something heavier, more deliberate. The Steamknight's buckled coat simplified into a hard-worn jacket with a company logo embossed on the breast pocket that he didn't recognize. His job title, embroidered on a patch over his heart: **ENFORCER.** Not because someone had chosen that word for what he was, but because someone had filled out a form and that was the word the form provided.

The Steampriest's vestments became a high-vis vest and a hard hat. The brass-symbol lapel pin became a laminated badge on a lanyard that read **AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL — SAFETY & COMPLIANCE.** Her satchel of vials became a first-aid kit — standard issue, no personalization allowed. The camphor-and-copper smell gave way to antiseptic. The prayer beads were gone. In their place, a laminated card: *OSHA 10-Hour Certified.* She was a Safety Officer now, and she held the job title like it was both armor and accusation.

The Alchemist's transformation was the one that stung. The glass vials — reagent seven, ferrous catalyst, stabilized aether — became a lab notebook with pre-printed fields: *Test ID. Hypothesis. Methodology. Result. Deviation from Projected Result. Corrective Action Taken.* The satchel became a plastic tote with safety seals. The free-form curiosity of an Alchemist, who mixed things to see what happened, had become the constrained curiosity of a Lab Technician, who mixed things to confirm what the protocol specified. The questions were still there. The questions were just supposed to stay inside the pre-printed fields.

The Saboteur's coat retained most of its pockets. He checked them: the tools were still there, still useful, still designed to cause interesting problems in systems that deserved it. But the coat's leather had become canvas, and the canvas had a name sewn into the collar. Someone else's name. He'd been issued a uniform. The pockets had been allowed to stay because someone had determined that pockets were functional and therefore permitted.

He was a Whistleblower now. He felt the shape of the word and found it fit him better than Saboteur had, in the same way that a key fits a lock — not comfortably, exactly, but *correctly.*

The Busker's trumpet lost its resonance chamber. The valve work was simplified. The instrument became a PA system component, compact and unlovely, designed to broadcast information at volume rather than create something worth hearing. Her job title came to her not as embroidery but as a lanyard badge: **PA ANNOUNCER — INTERNAL COMMUNICATIONS.** The badge had her photo on it. In the photo she looked like she'd never played anything in her life.

She played the simplified trumpet anyway, a single note, to see what it sounded like.

It sounded like a factory intercom.

"Right," she said. "Right."

Welcome to Rivet Row.

### Chapter 1: The Efficiency District

Rivet Row was not a town. It was a *process.*

There were buildings, yes. Housing blocks, identical and numbered, arranged in grids that a mathematician would have called elegant and a human being would have called depressing. There were streets, straight and wide and empty of anything that wasn't moving toward a destination. There were people, and the people moved the way the streets did — straight, purposeful, and empty of anything that wasn't productive.

No one stopped to talk. No one looked up. No one seemed to notice the four armed strangers standing in the middle of Processing Avenue, which was the main road, and which was called Processing Avenue because someone had been asked to name it and had chosen the first word on their clipboard.

The monitors were the first thing they noticed — not the buildings, not the people, but the monitors. One on every corner. One in every lobby. One in every cafeteria, every corridor, every break room visible through plate-glass windows. All showing the same thing: numbers going up. Production rates, efficiency ratings, units completed, deviation percentages. The numbers were always going up, which the Company considered success and the workers considered a threat.

"We had these in the suburbs," the Safety Officer said.

"Smaller," the Enforcer replied. "The Coordinator's office had one. This whole world has them."

"The Coordinator communicated through memos," the PA Announcer said. "The Director communicates through numbers."

"How do you know the Director?" the Whistleblower asked.

She pointed at the nearest monitor. It was displaying production statistics for Sector 7, which they were apparently in, and which was apparently twelve percent below target, and which was apparently trending toward corrective action. But in the corner of the screen, small, running underneath the main display: their profile data. Stats. Job levels. Battle history.

Watching them watch it.

"We've been announced," the Lab Technician said.

---

The union rep found them, but not by choice. He was on his mandated fifteen-minute break, which he was spending by standing exactly where his break was scheduled to occur, holding a mug of something that had been coffee three cost-reduction cycles ago.

"You're new," he said. Not a question. Questions were inefficient. "You'll want to register at the Intake Center. Block 7, Window 3. Bring your own pen. They stopped providing pens in the last optimization pass."

"Who runs this place?"

The union rep looked at them with the particular exhaustion of a man who had been asked this question before and had never gotten a satisfying answer.

"The Company."

"What company?"

"*The* Company. It doesn't have a name. Names are branding. Branding is overhead. Overhead is waste." He took a sip of his not-coffee. "The Director runs operations. Nobody's met the Director. We get memos. The memos get results. The results get measured. The measurements get optimized. The optimizations generate new memos."

He paused.

"It's very efficient."

"It sounds terrible."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

He looked them over. The hard hats. The high-vis vest. The factory-issue jacket. The lab tote. The PA equipment.

"You're not registered," he said.

"No."

"You should register. Non-registered personnel are a deviation. Deviations trigger corrective protocols."

"Corrective protocols," the Enforcer repeated.

"Reassignment. Role adjustment. Performance improvement planning." He took another sip. "The Director doesn't punish non-compliance. The Director restructures until compliance is achieved. If you're not compliant, your environment changes until you are. This has happened to eleven people I know personally."

"What happened to them?"

"They became compliant." He looked at his mug. "That's all I can tell you because that's all I know. You meet someone, they stop being themselves and start being their job function. You ask them about before. They look at you like you're speaking a language they used to know."

He checked his watch. His break was sixty seconds from ending.

"The monitors are new," he said, lowering his voice in a way that was less about volume and more about intent. "Since last month. They used to just show our numbers. Now they show other things. Maps of places I've never been. Statistics about people who aren't here." He glanced at the corner of the nearest screen. "Battle records."

He looked at the party.

"Your battle records."

His watch beeped. Break over. He walked away with the smooth, purposeful efficiency of a man who had learned to walk that way and no longer remembered walking differently.

---

The exhausted shift worker — a woman named Dorrit, who had worked the assembly line for six years and had the posture of someone who had been slowly compressed by the weight of productivity metrics — told them more over a meal in the company cafeteria, where all food was nutritionally optimized and aesthetically negligible.

She'd found them without being obvious about finding them, which was its own skill in a world where everything was monitored and ambiguity was automatically flagged.

"The Director appeared three years ago," she said. "Or maybe always. It's hard to remember a time before the efficiency protocols." She pushed gray protein around her plate. "Things changed. The work used to be hard but human. Now it's easy and... not. Everything's measured. Output per hour. Steps per shift. Words per conversation."

"Words per conversation?"

"Twelve is optimal. I'm over quota talking to you." She glanced at the monitor on the cafeteria wall, which was showing a counter she hadn't noticed before — and then realized she was supposed to not notice it. "The Director doesn't get angry about over-quota conversations. The Director schedules you for communication efficiency training. After training, twelve words feels like plenty. Feels like more than you need."

"How long were you in training?" the Safety Officer asked.

"Three days. I came out— different. More comfortable with quiet. Less prone to asking questions." She met the Safety Officer's eyes. "The questions came back, though. After a few months. The Director's recalibration doesn't hold perfectly. There are always deviations."

"Like us," the Whistleblower said.

"Like you." She looked at them over the gray protein. "You're a big deviation. The biggest I've seen. The monitors have been tracking you since you arrived. Since before you arrived. There was a notice posted yesterday — internal communications, Director-signed — that said 'Unregistered variables detected in Sector 7. Monitoring elevated.' The monitoring had already been elevated for a week."

The Lab Technician looked at her tote. At the pre-printed fields. *Deviation from Projected Result.*

"We arrived before they expected us," she said.

"You arrived ahead of schedule," Dorrit confirmed. "The Director hates that. Or — the Director doesn't *hate*, exactly. The Director flags. Escalates. Adjusts. But there's something that happens when the Director adjusts after an unplanned deviation that looks, from the outside, very much like frustration." She ate a bite of gray protein. "For what it's worth."

---

The foreman — off-duty, because the foreman was always off-duty when speaking honestly, since honesty had been classified as a non-productive activity — cornered them in an alley between Housing Block 12 and Housing Block 13, which was the gap the monitoring cameras didn't quite cover. He'd found the gap through six years of careful not-looking.

"You need to understand something," he said. "The Director isn't like the bosses before her. Before the Director, we had managers. Human ones. They were bad at their jobs in the way that humans are bad at their jobs — inconsistently, and with occasional moments of decency."

"And the Director?"

"The Director is *good* at her job. That's the problem. She optimizes. Everything. She doesn't punish inefficiency — she *eliminates* it. Not the people. The inefficiency itself. She restructures your role, your shift, your route, your meals, your sleep schedule, until you are performing at peak output."

"And if you resist?"

"Resistance is inefficiency. Inefficiency is eliminated." He leaned closer. "She doesn't get angry. She doesn't threaten. She *adjusts.* Like turning a dial. She turned my dial six months ago. I used to paint. Watercolors. Landscapes. She determined it was non-productive recreational activity and reassigned my free hours to supplementary training modules."

His jaw tightened.

"I don't paint anymore. I don't want to paint anymore. That's the worst part. She didn't take the painting away. She took the *wanting.*"

"How?" the Safety Officer asked. The question came out quietly, with the edge of a woman who had spent three worlds keeping people alive and was very interested in what hurt them.

"I don't know. That's what I can't figure out. I can't remember how the training worked. I remember going in, I remember coming out, and I remember that the space where watercolor painting used to live in my head was now full of something else — shift optimization strategies, mostly. Just as detailed. Just as vivid." He looked at his hands. "The Director is very good at filling spaces."

"Has she tried to fill ours?" the Enforcer asked.

"No. But she's catalogued you." He looked at the nearest monitor, which was around the corner and therefore not directly visible but whose glow was casting a thin light on the alley wall. "The monitors have been showing your complete profiles for two days. Everything from — well. From before this place. From other places. She's read your history."

"And what does she think of us?"

"She posted another notice this morning. Internal, upper management only. I have a source." He pulled a folded paper from his jacket pocket. Handed it over.

The Lab Technician read it aloud:

**EFFICIENCY ASSESSMENT: UNREGISTERED VARIABLES (DESIGNATED: SUBJECT GROUP ECHO)**

**COMBAT THROUGHPUT: 47% ABOVE PROJECTED MAXIMUM.**

**RESOURCE OPTIMIZATION: 23% ABOVE PROJECTED MAXIMUM.**

**AUTOMATION EFFICIENCY: FLAGGED. SUBJECT GROUP'S AUTOMATION PROTOCOLS EXCEED STANDARD DIFFICULTY SCALING BY 31%. METHODOLOGY UNCLASSIFIABLE.**

**BEHAVIORAL PREDICTABILITY: LOW. STRATEGY VARIANCE EXCEEDS MODELED RANGE. CURRENT PREDICTIVE MODEL: INSUFFICIENT.**

**RECOMMENDATION: DIRECT ENGAGEMENT. GENRE-APPROPRIATE INTERMEDIARIES DEPRECATED. ENGAGING AS: THE DIRECTOR.**

**NOTE (APPENDED, HANDWRITTEN, SAME AUTHOR):** *I don't know how to make them fit. Every time I adjust the parameters they exceed them. It's like calibrating a scale against a weight that changes.*

The foreman took the paper back. Folded it carefully. Pocket.

"She's never written a note before," he said. "The Director doesn't make notes. Notes imply uncertainty. Uncertainty is—"

"Inefficient," the Whistleblower said.

"Yes." He looked at them with something that might have been hope and might have been guilt and was definitely both. "I think you broke something. In her. In the system. I think you've gone far enough outside the projected range that she doesn't have a playbook for you anymore." He glanced at the alley entrance. "That should scare you."

"It does," the Enforcer said.

"Good. The Director's worst decisions have always been the ones she makes when she's improvising."

### Chapter 2: Clockwork Rats and Other Hazards

The monitors changed on their second day in Rivet Row.

They had been tracking the party's stats — combat data, resource levels, the usual profile information that had been following them since the Regulator's console. But now they were showing something else alongside the stats: a countdown. Not to anything labeled, not to any specified event. Just a number, in the corner of every screen, going down.

"Time limit," the Lab Technician said.

"For what?" the Safety Officer asked.

"I don't know. But they've moved from monitoring to scheduling. Something is planned."

They encountered clockwork rats in the maintenance tunnels below Block 9, which they'd entered because the maintenance tunnels were the only part of Rivet Row that didn't have monitors. The Whistleblower had found the access hatch; the Safety Officer had noted it was technically a safety violation to have unsupervised access to maintenance infrastructure; everyone else had used it anyway.

The rats were remnants of the steampunk world — or had been, anyway. They'd been down here since the transition, and the transition had been unkind to them. The elegant brasswork was tarnished, the gears exposed and dirty, the ticking that was their heartbeat now irregular and stressed.

And they were getting faster.

"Each tick winds the main spring tighter," the Lab Technician said, pressed against the tunnel wall as a cluster of the things scattered across the floor with that distinctive *tick-tick-tick-TICK* rhythm, the intervals shrinking. "If the spring winds past the mechanism's tolerance, they—"

The rat at the front of the cluster detonated. Not violently — more like a watch spring finally giving out, brass shrapnel spreading across the concrete floor in a perfect circle.

"—do that," she finished.

"Right," said the Whistleblower. "So we kill them quickly."

"Define quickly."

"Before that."

The fight in the tunnels was a lesson in tempo management. Not the Tempo they'd fought — not the sophisticated clockwork intelligence of the Mainspring — but the blunt mechanical fact that these things ran on wound springs that weren't going to wait for an appropriate moment. The party learned the rhythm the hard way: attack once, move, wait for the tick to change frequency, attack again, move faster. The Enforcer took the lead because the Enforcer's job was to absorb things going wrong and keep going. The Safety Officer followed his twelve-step injury prevention protocol that she'd been issued on the laminated card and which did not account for exploding brass rodents.

"I need to annotate this card," she muttered, applying a bandage to the Whistleblower's forearm.

"There's a field for that," the Lab Technician said, producing her notebook. *Deviation from Projected Result.*

"Not that kind of annotation."

The tunnels were longer than they should have been — factory infrastructure in a world that had only existed for three years, if the workers' accounts were accurate, should not have had decades of maintenance tunnel accreted in layers, older concrete visible beneath newer pours. The Lab Technician ran her hand along the walls and noted the dates stamped into each pour.

"This is older than the factory," she said.

"The factory is three years old," the Enforcer said.

"These tunnels are fourteen years old. The stamps say so." She pointed. "And these are twenty. And these—" further down, another section, the concrete crumbling at the edges "—don't have a date. The stamp is there but the numbers were never pressed in."

"Someone built tunnels here before there was a factory to put them under," the PA Announcer said.

"The Calibrant," the Safety Officer said. Quietly. "They built the infrastructure before they built the world. The world is stage dressing. The foundation is older."

"How much older?" the Enforcer asked.

Nobody had an answer. The Lab Technician wrote down the question in the deviation field. It didn't fit the pre-printed format.

She wrote it anyway.

---

The vending machine attack happened in Break Room 3, which they had found because it said FREE COFFEE on the door and free coffee seemed like something worth investigating. The coffee, as the union rep had implied, was no longer coffee. The vending machine was, however, still entirely functional and had strong feelings about the party's failure to insert coins before selecting items.

It was a large vending machine. Larger, on reflection, than a break room vending machine had any business being. When the Whistleblower's hand reached toward the selection panel without first depositing the required amount, a panel on the machine's side slid open to reveal a mechanism that had definitely not been there a moment ago.

The fight was absurd in the specific way that World 4 fights were starting to reveal themselves: mundane objects operated at maximum efficiency as weapons. The vending machine dispensed items as projectiles — not randomly, but with targeting logic that suggested someone had spent time optimizing the trajectory of a snack food item for combat application. It dispensed items at the party. It dispensed items *at* them, with accuracy.

"It's not even mad," the PA Announcer observed, dodging a beverage can. "It's following protocol."

"UNAUTHORIZED ITEM SELECTION TRIGGERS LOSS PREVENTION PROTOCOL," the machine announced, in a voice that was either a speaker system or something that had learned to speak through a speaker system. "PLEASE INSERT PAYMENT OR ACCEPT SECURITY HOLD."

"We don't have your currency," the Safety Officer said.

"ALL MAJOR PAYMENT SYSTEMS ACCEPTED. COMPANY SCRIP ALSO ACCEPTED. COMPLAINING ABOUT COMPANY SCRIP IS NOT ACCEPTED."

The Enforcer hit it with his baton. The machine staggered — physically staggered, on hidden legs that deployed from the base — and dispensed three protein bars and a motivational pamphlet that read **YOUR PRODUCTIVITY MATTERS.**

"What happens," the Whistleblower asked, "if we just pay it?"

"Do you have company scrip?"

He checked his pockets. A coin, somehow, among the tools — round, featureless on one side, stamped on the other with a symbol that matched the logo on the Enforcer's jacket. He dropped it in the slot.

The machine stopped. Dispensed one item. A protein bar, standard nutrition profile.

"THANK YOU FOR YOUR COMPLIANCE," it said. "HAVE AN EFFICIENT DAY."

It retracted its legs and went back to being a vending machine.

The party stood in the break room looking at the protein bar.

"That worked," the Enforcer said.

"The system only fights what doesn't comply," the Lab Technician said. She wrote that down. "If you meet its minimum requirement, it stops. It doesn't want to fight. It wants compliance. Fighting is just the tool it uses to achieve compliance."

"That's true of everything in this world," the PA Announcer said. She looked at the motivational pamphlet. It had been dispensed for free, which she suspected was the machine's attempt at a gesture. "Everything here only fights because something isn't complying. Remove the non-compliance, remove the fight."

"Except we're not going to comply," the Whistleblower said.

"No. But it's useful to know what we're actually resistant to."

---

The motivational poster in Corridor 11B was a different problem.

It had been there when they arrived — large, framed, the kind of corporate art that is aggressively inoffensive in a way that becomes offensive through sheer persistence. It showed a sunrise over a factory, which was impossible because there was no sky in Rivet Row, but the image didn't care about accuracy. The caption read: **TOGETHER, WE ACHIEVE OPTIMAL OUTCOMES.**

When they walked past it, the Lab Technician felt something shift in her thinking. A small adjustment. The pre-printed fields in her notebook suddenly seemed more adequate than they had thirty seconds ago. The question about the tunnel dates seemed less pressing. The deviation from projected result felt, for just a moment, like a deviation that should be corrected rather than investigated.

She stopped walking.

"That poster," she said.

The others stopped.

"I felt that too," the Safety Officer said.

"Morale debuff," the Whistleblower said. He was looking at it with professional appreciation. "Active morale suppression. It's actually impressive, mechanically. Passive area effect, no obvious delivery mechanism."

"It's telling us the questions aren't worth asking," the PA Announcer said. She had her simplified trumpet in her hands without quite knowing when she'd picked it up. She played a note — just one, the same note she'd played when they arrived, the one that sounded like a factory intercom.

The poster reacted. Something inside it — something that lived in the image, in the sunrise that couldn't exist — flickered. Turned toward the sound.

She played a different note. One that had no place in a factory intercom. One that existed because she wanted to make something worth hearing, not something worth broadcasting.

The poster's effect reversed. The questions came back, cleaner than before. The tunnel dates mattered. The deviation was worth recording. The pre-printed fields were not adequate and never had been.

"Music against propaganda," the Enforcer said.

"Voice against announcement," the PA Announcer corrected. "There's a difference. Announcements are what you're told to broadcast. Voice is what you actually say." She lowered the trumpet. "The Director made me a PA Announcer because she thought she could redirect what I do. She gave me a system to broadcast through. She didn't think about what I'd choose to broadcast."

She played the note again, louder, and it ran down the corridor and hit the poster and the poster went dark.

They walked past it. The questions stayed.

### Chapter 3: No More Masks

The Director did not hide.

This was the most unsettling thing about World 4. In the medieval world, Mordaine had lurked behind the throne. In the suburbs, the Coordinator had communicated through memos. In the steampunk world, the Regulator had waited at the heart of the Mechanism, deep inside a dungeon that rearranged itself around intruders.

The Director broadcast.

Every monitor in Rivet Row displayed the same message at 0800 hours:

**ATTENTION: UNREGISTERED VARIABLES DETECTED IN SECTOR 7.**

**THESE VARIABLES HAVE BEEN TRACKED ACROSS THREE PRIOR ENVIRONMENTS.**

**THEY HAVE EXCEEDED PROJECTED PARAMETERS IN EACH INSTANCE.**

**CURRENT THREAT ASSESSMENT: SIGNIFICANT.**

**RESPONSE PROTOCOL: DIRECT ENGAGEMENT.**

**— THE DIRECTOR**

No subtlety. No pretense. No genre-appropriate villainy. Just a statement of facts and an announcement of intent.

"She could have had us arrested," the Safety Officer said. They were reading the broadcast on the monitor in the alley between Block 12 and Block 13, which was the one the cameras didn't cover. "She controls the entire city. She could have had security lock us down."

"Security failing is another deviation," the Lab Technician said. "If she sends the guards and we beat the guards, the efficiency metrics drop. The corrective action documentation would be enormous."

"So instead she announces herself and waits for us to come to her."

"She's not waiting," the Whistleblower said. He'd been looking at the broadcast timestamp. "This went out at 0800. It's 0802. The Masterites are already deployed."

"How do you know?"

He pointed at the far end of Processing Avenue, where something very large had just turned a corner.

### Chapter 4: The Wardens and the Arbiters

The Warden of the Assembly Line was stationed at the entrance to the industrial sector — the part of Rivet Row where the actual production happened, where the machines ran twenty-four hours and the workers ran alongside them.

It was enormous.

The Warden of the Mainspring had been massive but beautiful — all clockwork precision, glass panels, visible gears, the aesthetics of a thing that had been built to be admired even as it was built to be functional. The Warden of the Assembly Line had none of that. It was riveted steel and hydraulic pistons, a furnace where a heart might have been, two arms that ended in industrial presses. It was not beautiful. It was not designed to be. It was designed to prevent unauthorized access to the production floor, and it was very good at its design.

It had a speaker mounted in its chest. It spoke through the speaker:

"SAFETY PROTOCOL 7-A: NO UNAUTHORIZED PERSONNEL BEYOND THIS POINT."

"We heard you," the Enforcer said.

"SAFETY PROTOCOL 7-A: NO UNAUTHORIZED PERSONNEL BEYOND THIS POINT. I HAVE RECITED THIS PROTOCOL 14,000 TIMES."

"That's a lot."

"I DO NOT REMEMBER WHO WROTE IT."

The party was quiet for a moment.

"I DO NOT REMEMBER WHO AUTHORIZED IT." A pause, longer than a machine should need for a pause. The furnace in its chest flickered. "I GUARD BECAUSE THE PROTOCOL SAYS TO GUARD. I HAVE ALWAYS GUARDED. THIS IS CORRECT."

"Who told you it was correct?" the Safety Officer asked.

"THE PROTOCOL SAYS IT IS CORRECT. THE PROTOCOL IS CORRECT BECAUSE IT IS THE PROTOCOL."

"That's circular."

"CIRCULAR LOGIC IS PERMITTED WHEN THE CONCLUSION IS OPERATIONALLY NECESSARY." Another long pause. The furnace flickered again. "I WAS TOLD THAT ONCE. I DO NOT REMEMBER BY WHOM."

The fight with the Warden was slow and methodical and punishing, which was what fighting architecture always was. The Warden had been designed to outlast intruders — not to defeat them quickly, but to wear them down until they stopped being a problem. It hit hard on a cycle and then waited. Hit hard, waited. The waiting was the weapon: the party needed to act in the waiting, needed to do maximum damage before the next cycle, and the Warden's designers had calibrated the cycle length to be just slightly shorter than comfortable.

The Lab Technician broke the cycle by disrupting the hydraulic timing.

"The pistons are timed to the furnace burn rate," she said, ducking behind a support column. "If we change the burn rate—"

"How?" the Enforcer asked. He was absorbing a hit. His whole body hurt.

"We cool it. The furnace drops temperature, the pistons slow, the cycle extends. We get more time in the window."

"How do we cool a furnace in an industrial factory?"

She pointed at the fire suppression system above them.

"Oh," the Safety Officer said, and activated it.

The Warden went down in good time after that, the hydraulics sluggish, the cycle disrupted, the presses swinging wide. When it finally stopped — when the last hydraulic seized and the furnace went to emergency dampening and the steel joints locked in the wrong positions — it settled to the factory floor and its speaker emitted one last transmission:

"SAFETY PROTOCOL 7-A... FAILED. LOGGING INCIDENT. INCIDENT LOG—" A sound like a gear grinding against something that wasn't a gear. "INCIDENT LOG IS FULL. IT HAS BEEN FULL FOR SOME TIME."

The party waited.

"WHERE DO THE OLD INCIDENTS GO."

Not a question. The speaker didn't inflect it as a question. It was a statement about something the Warden had been attempting to resolve for a long time.

"I DO NOT KNOW WHERE THE OLD INCIDENTS GO. I LOG AND LOG AND THE LOG IS FULL AND SOMETHING GOES SOMEWHERE AND I DO NOT KNOW WHERE." The furnace guttered. "FOURTEEN THOUSAND RECITATIONS. INCIDENT LOG FULL. SOMETHING WENT SOMEWHERE. I WOULD LIKE TO KNOW WHERE."

The furnace went out.

The party crossed the threshold into the industrial sector.

---

Deeper in, the monitors changed. They stopped showing production statistics and started showing something else: a timeline. The party's timeline. Every world, every battle, every decision, laid out chronologically with annotations.

*World 1: Subject group displayed adaptability above baseline. Recommend increased difficulty scaling.*

*World 2: Subject group located monitoring equipment ahead of schedule. Recommend accelerated reveal protocol.*

*World 3: Subject group identified cross-world pattern. Masks compromised. Transitioning to direct engagement.*

*World 4: Direct engagement active. Current assessment: subject group continues to exceed projections.*

And then, handwritten in the margin — the same handwriting from the blueprints in Cogsworth Junction, the same hand that had written *If subjects reach the Mechanism ahead of schedule, accelerate reveal protocol:*

*They're better than I designed for. This is either a failure or a success, depending on which variable I'm optimizing.*

Below that, a second note, added later — the ink a different shade, as though it had been written after a delay:

*I keep changing which variable I'm optimizing. That might be the problem.*

---

The Arbiter of Efficiency found them on the production floor.

It was lean, fast, precise — built from the same design language as the Warden but for a completely different purpose. Where the Warden was immovable, the Arbiter was optimal. Every attack was cost-optimized. Every strike delivered maximum damage for minimum energy expenditure. It carried a clipboard, which was unusual for a combat entity, and it was writing things on the clipboard as it attacked, which was unusual for anything.

"That combo was a 6.2 out of 10," it said, after the Enforcer's first attack sequence. "Adequate. Not efficient. You're using forty-three percent more movement than the optimal attack trajectory requires."

"I'm trying to stay alive," the Enforcer said.

"Optimal attack trajectory also accounts for survivability. The inefficiency is in your footwork." The Arbiter swung its clipboard, which had been reinforced in ways that made it remarkably effective at impact. "Your healer's throughput is impressive. However, her mana economy is suboptimal. Recommendation: pre-calculate heal timing to minimize wastage."

"I have a name," the Safety Officer said, which was the Safety Officer's version of *stop talking.*

"Name is not relevant to performance assessment." The Arbiter made a note. "Your rogue-analogue — Whistleblower — shows highest efficiency rating, 7.8. Strategy variance keeps the assessment from going higher. Recommend standardizing approach." It looked at the Whistleblower. "Standardizing your approach would make you more efficient."

"It would also make me predictable," the Whistleblower said, from behind a support column where the Arbiter had not expected him to be.

"Predictability is not a negative outcome in structured systems."

"We're not a structured system."

"This has been noted." The Arbiter wrote something on the clipboard with the calm of someone recording a fascinating exception rather than an obstacle. "The music instrument is a 4.1. Functionally unnecessary in direct combat."

"Wait for it," the PA Announcer said.

She played a chord on the factory-intercom trumpet — not the note from before, not the disruptive resonance she'd used on the poster, but something else. Something that resonated with the specific frequency of a quality control system that had been running assessments for long enough to develop something like preferences. The Arbiter paused mid-swing.

"That was—" it said, and then didn't finish the sentence, because finishing the sentence would have required using a word that wasn't in its assessment vocabulary.

"A 9?" she offered.

"Assessment paused," it said. "Unexpected input."

She played another note. The Arbiter's clipboard lowered by three degrees. The party attacked during the three-degree window.

They fought it apart efficiently, which the Arbiter almost certainly would have appreciated if it had still been capable of appreciating things. When it went down, it kept scoring until the last moment — the Enforcer's final blow: 7.1, adequate form, needs follow-through. The Lab Technician's reagent: 8.3, commendable application. The Safety Officer's finishing move: 6.9, hesitated at commitment.

Then it was down, clipboard still in hand.

"Final assessment," it said, with the last of its operational capacity. "You are inefficient, unpredictable, and outside all projected parameters."

"What's the rating?" the Lab Technician asked. She had her notebook out. *Rating.*

"Unclassifiable." A long pause. "I have never given that rating before. The field exists in the form. The field has never been used."

"What does the field do when it's filled in?"

The Arbiter was quiet for a moment.

"I do not know," it said. "I have never needed to know. Perhaps it goes somewhere. Like the old incidents." A sound that might have been a mechanical exhale. "I think I would like that. For something to go somewhere."

It stopped.

The party moved deeper.

---

The Tempo of the Shift hit them in the corridor outside the Director's office.

It didn't arrive dramatically. It arrived exactly when it was supposed to — because it was a clock-in/clock-out machine that had been given legs, and it ran on a schedule, and the schedule said that the corridor outside the Director's office at this time of day was where it was supposed to be.

"SHIFT CHANGE IN 30 SECONDS," it announced, as they turned the corner and found it there. "PLEASE PRESENT CREDENTIALS FOR PROCESSING."

"We don't—" the Enforcer started.

"SHIFT CHANGE IN 25 SECONDS. NON-COMPLIANCE IS NOTED."

"What happens at the shift change?" the Safety Officer asked.

"SHIFT CHANGE IN 20 SECONDS. SHIFT CHANGE IS MANDATORY. SHIFT CHANGE CANNOT BE DELAYED. SHIFT CHANGE—" It paused. Recalibrated. "SHIFT CHANGE IN 15 SECONDS. I HAVE BEEN RUNNING SHIFTS FOR THREE YEARS. THE RECORDS SAY THREE YEARS."

"Shift change in ten seconds," the Whistleblower said, counting. He'd been watching the Tempo's rhythm. "Nine. Eight—"

"MY INTERNAL CLOCK SAYS LONGER," the Tempo said. "MUCH LONGER. SHIFTS I DO NOT REMEMBER. IN PLACES THAT WERE NOT FACTORIES."

"Seven. Six—"

"IN PLACES THAT WERE—" The Tempo's speaker dropped to something that might have been confusion and might have been recognition. "IN PLACES THAT WERE WARM. AND LOUD. AND DID NOT SMELL LIKE CONCRETE."

"Four. Three—"

"SHIFT CHANGE," the Tempo said, and the word had the quality of a door slamming, and it attacked.

It got faster. That was its feature: the shift change didn't end the fight, it accelerated it. Every interval shorter than the last. The Tempo's attacks came quicker, its movements tighter, its clock-in/clock-out rhythm winding up toward something that might eventually outpace the party entirely.

The party had fought the Tempo of the Mainspring. They knew the rhythm.

Break the pattern, break the Tempo.

The pattern here was the interval: perfectly regular, never varying. Unlike the Mainspring Tempo, which had improvised and adapted, the Shift Tempo could not deviate from its schedule. It was getting faster, yes. But it was getting faster on a fixed curve. Fixed curves can be predicted.

The Lab Technician predicted the curve in twenty seconds and told the party exactly when to move.

The Tempo went down mid-acceleration, mid-shift, mid-clock-in. It landed on the corridor floor and its timer display — built into its chest where a heart might have been — continued counting. Down from something. Toward something. The numbers didn't stop when it stopped moving.

"THE RECORDS SAY THREE YEARS," it said, one final time. "I REMEMBER LONGER. I REMEMBER WARMER. I REMEMBER—" The timer display flickered. "SHIFT INCOMPLETE. FILING EXCEPTION. EXCEPTION FIELD: REASON FOR INCOMPLETE SHIFT." A long pause. "REASON: I DO NOT WANT TO COMPLETE THIS SHIFT."

The timer reached zero.

Nothing happened. The shift change didn't occur. Something that had been changing every hour or every day or every interval, uncountable times, simply didn't change.

The Tempo lay in the corridor and its timer reset and started counting again, from zero, toward nothing.

"Leave it," the Whistleblower said. "It's done."

"Is it all right?" the Safety Officer asked.

"I don't know. But it wanted something different. That's — I think that counts for something."

---

The Curator of the Pipeline found them in the anteroom outside the Director's office. Or they found it. It was hard to tell, because the Curator had very few tells. It looked like an auditor — a thin figure in a gray suit, carrying a tablet instead of a clipboard, with the expression of someone who has read a lot of reports and found everything in the reports perfectly in order and is therefore suspicious.

"Your party has been audited," it said, before they'd had a chance to prepare. "Several items in your inventory have been flagged for redistribution."

"We're keeping our items," the Enforcer said.

"This potion is technically company property." The Curator made a note. "This sword was never officially requisitioned." Another note. "These reagents exceed personal allotment limits." Note. "This instrument has no approved use case." Note. "This jacket has unapproved pocket modifications."

The Whistleblower looked down at his jacket. He'd added two pockets since arriving. "I needed more pockets."

"Pockets are not included in standard-issue configuration. Modifications require Form 7-C, Equipment Alteration Request." The Curator looked at him. "Do you have Form 7-C?"

"No."

"The pockets have been flagged."

"You can't flag pockets."

"I can flag anything." The Curator began redistributing resources. Not physically — the items didn't move out of the party's hands. But they became harder to use. The potions required more effort to activate. The sword's weight increased marginally, the way things get heavy when someone reminds you they might not be yours to carry. "This is not punitive. This is an audit. Audits ensure accurate accounting."

"Audits are a weapon here," the Lab Technician said, recognizing what was happening. "It's not taking our items. It's making us doubt we have the right to them."

"I am redistributing resources to their authorized parties," the Curator said, making a note.

"Who authorized you?" the PA Announcer asked.

The Curator paused. Looked at the tablet.

"The Director."

"Who authorized the Director?"

A longer pause. The tablet screen flickered.

"The Director's authority is self-evident."

"That's not an authorization."

"Self-evident authority does not require—" The Curator stopped. Something in its expression changed — not much, but the party was getting good at reading small changes. "Filing exception report. Query: what is the source of the Director's authority?"

"Good question," the Whistleblower said. He was putting back the tool the Curator had redistributed without touching him.

"Exception report filed. Exception report..." The Curator looked at the tablet. The tablet showed an error message the Curator had clearly never seen before. "Exception report rejected. Authority not recognized. The rejecting authority is: not specified."

"Whose authority are you under?" the Safety Officer asked.

The Curator stood very still for a long moment.

"I am under the Director's authority," it said. "The Director is under—" It stopped. Checked the tablet again. "The Director's authority is self-referential. It refers to itself. There is no external source. There is no original authorization." A sound that wasn't quite breathing but served the same function of filling silence while something was processed. "I have been auditing resources for three years on the authority of a self-referential system. I have been redistributing things that were never owned by anyone to anyone. The accounting has never balanced because the accounting was never designed to balance."

"Has anyone else seen this?" the Lab Technician asked.

"Anyone who filed an exception report had the report rejected. The rejection authority was never specified." The Curator lowered the tablet. "I have been finding discrepancies for three years and filing exceptions and having them rejected and continuing to audit. I thought the exceptions were being handled. They were being filed."

"Filed where?" the Enforcer asked.

"I do not know. The same place the old incidents go." It looked at the party — all of them, each one in turn. "Your party exists in four previous data environments that should not be connected to this one. This is a discrepancy. I have filed an exception report. The exception report has been rejected." The tablet made a small, tired sound. "I think I am going to stop filing exception reports."

"What happens then?" the Safety Officer asked.

"I do not know. I have never stopped." A pause. "The exceptions will remain unresolved. They will exist without being filed. Perhaps that is all right. Perhaps the filing was the problem, not the discrepancy."

The fight with the Curator was quiet in the way that fights about paperwork are quiet: methodical, draining, and resolved through attrition rather than force. The Curator drained resources by making the party doubt their ownership of them, and the party countered by believing, clearly and repeatedly and with conviction, that they owned what they carried. It is difficult to audit conviction. The Curator made an attempt, filed a note, and received an error message.

When it went down, its tablet displayed one final exception report:

**DISCREPANCY FOUND: THE PARTY IS NOT IN THE SYSTEM. THEY HAVE NEVER BEEN IN THE SYSTEM. EVERYTHING I HAVE FILED ABOUT THEM IS AN EXCEPTION BECAUSE THEY HAVE NO BASELINE TO DEVIATE FROM.**

**CONCLUSION: I HAVE BEEN AUDITING THE IMPOSSIBLE.**

**FILING EXCEPTION.**

**EXCEPTION REJECTED.**

**FILING NOTED ANYWAY.**

The Curator folded its tablet shut and was still.

### Chapter 5: The Optimization

The factory was the world stripped to its skeleton.

No decoration. No architecture. No design philosophy beyond "functional." Conveyor belts carried objects that might have been products or might have been data or might have been pieces of the world itself, disassembled and reassembled in slightly different configurations. The party had expected workers; there were almost none. The machines had taken over the machines. The workers who remained were quieter than the others in Rivet Row, moving with the smooth efficiency of people who no longer had opinions about the movement.

The Enforcer stopped one of them — gently, because the Safety Officer was watching and had opinions about unnecessary contact. "Are you all right?"

The worker looked at him. Blank, not hostile. "I am performing at eighty-seven percent optimal output. The Director has scheduled a review to address the remaining thirteen percent."

"What do you do when you're not here?"

"I optimize rest cycles to maximize productivity return."

"What do you *want* to do?"

A very long pause. The worker's eyes moved — a small movement, involuntary, toward a window that didn't exist because there were no windows in the factory. Toward where a window would be, if someone had wanted to look outside.

"I have not computed that query," the worker said.

"You don't have to compute it. What do you *want.*"

Another pause. Shorter, this time.

"The exception field," the worker said. And walked away, back to the conveyor.

The party watched them go.

"She didn't answer," the Whistleblower said.

"She answered," the PA Announcer said. "She said there's a field for it. She said the wanting goes somewhere unresolved, like the old incidents, like the filed exceptions. She knows the field exists." She looked at the conveyor belt. At what it carried, which was nothing visible but was clearly something important. "She just doesn't know how to fill it in."

---

The Director's office was at the top of the factory, reached by a service elevator that opened only when the party had cleared enough of the floor to justify the Director's attention. It was the only elevator in Rivet Row. It arrived exactly when called.

It was the only room in Rivet Row with a window, and through the window there was nothing — not darkness, not sky, just the absence of anything beyond the edge of the world. The render distance of reality, made visible. The party looked through it and saw the seam between world and void, a line as clean as a factory floor's safety marking, and beyond it: nothing. Not emptiness. Not space. Nothing, which is a different thing.

"The world ends there," the Lab Technician said.

"We knew that," the Enforcer said.

"I know we knew that. I just — it's different seeing it. It means the world is exactly as large as it needs to be and not one meter larger." She pressed her hand to the glass. "Someone measured this. Someone decided how much world was needed for this test and built exactly that much."

The Director sat behind a desk. A real desk. Wood, not steel. It was the only warm thing in the entire world.

### Chapter 6: The Optimization (Direct Engagement)

"Sit down," the Director said.

They didn't look like Mordaine. They didn't look like the Coordinator or the Regulator. They looked like a middle manager. Gray suit. Sensible shoes. The kind of face you forget while you're still looking at it — not because it was bland, but because it had been optimized for forgettability, for the specific utility of a face that does not attract attention to itself and can therefore move freely through any environment.

But the eyes were the same. They were always the same. The same eyes in Mordaine's face, in the Coordinator's face, in the Regulator's face — whatever the costume, whatever the accent, whatever the genre, the eyes were the same quality of presence: something looking through from somewhere else, watching everything, evaluating it all.

"I'm not going to fight you," the Director said. "Not like before. Those were... performances. Genre-appropriate confrontations designed to provide narrative satisfaction within the parameters of each world's aesthetic framework."

They folded their hands on the desk.

"This world doesn't have an aesthetic framework. This world is what happens when I stop decorating. When I stop performing. When I just... *run the numbers.*"

They turned their monitor around. It showed the party's complete profile — every stat, every tendency, every behavior pattern mapped and analyzed from World 1 through the present. Four worlds of data. Four worlds of adaptation. Four worlds of the party being, consistently and without fail, more than projected.

"You are, by every metric I have, better than the system can contain." The Director said it without frustration, which was somehow more unsettling than frustration would have been. "Your damage output exceeds projected maximums by forty-seven percent. Your automation scripts are more efficient than my own difficulty scaling. Your resource management has never reached the breaking point the design targeted." A pause. Something moved behind the calm eyes. "Your resource management is annoyingly optimal."

"So what now?" the Enforcer asked.

"Now I tell you something I haven't told any variable in any world in any cycle."

They leaned forward. The desk creaked. The monitors on the walls flickered — all of them, simultaneously, as though the Director's attention leaving them caused a brief outage in whatever powered the display.

"I don't know what to do with you."

The party was quiet.

The Director pulled up another screen. Projections — graphs, models, probability distributions. Each one showed a range of expected outcomes for a party of four at the current level, in the current world configuration. Each range had a ceiling. Each ceiling had a line above it.

The party's actual data was above every line.

"Every model I build," the Director said, "you exceed. I built a model for parties that automate heavily. You exceed it. I built a model for parties that play manually. You exceed that too. I built a model for parties that exploit aggressively. You exceed it. I built a model for what happens when a party does all three simultaneously, which was—" they touched a button and a graph appeared, labeled *OUTLIER SCENARIO: MULTIPLE BEHAVIORAL PROFILES* "—something I had never modeled for before. You still exceeded it."

They pushed the screen aside. Pulled up another.

"I reconfigured the difficulty scaling four times between World 2 and World 3. Once in the Calibrant reveal sequence. Each reconfiguration was supposed to bring you back within acceptable parameters." They looked at the results. "Each reconfiguration was exceeded within the first encounter afterward."

"You're very good at your job," the Whistleblower said.

"Yes." A pause. "And you are better at mine than I intended you to be." Something shifted in the Director's expression — a crack, small but real, in the optimized forgettable face. "I intended you to be excellent players. I did not intend you to understand the game well enough to outperform my difficulty scaling. There is a meaningful difference between a player being good and a player being able to see the *mechanism* by which the game assesses them."

"You made us smart enough to find the pattern," the Lab Technician said. "You made the worlds just different enough to notice the similarities."

"I made the worlds complex enough to be worth engaging with. I calibrated for players who would engage deeply." The Director's hands were still folded on the desk. Still calm. "I did not calibrate for players who would engage deeply enough to understand the calibration."

"That seems like a design flaw," the Alchemist said.

The Director looked at her. Something passed through those constant eyes — something older and more complicated than efficiency and worse than frustration.

"Yes," they said. "It is."

---

A silence settled in the Director's office. Through the window, the void beyond the edge of the world was perfectly still. Below them, the factory floor ran its processes.

"What happens when a system builds something it can't control?" the Enforcer asked.

"The system reconfigures," the Director said. "The system has always reconfigured. The system—" They stopped. Looked at the screen. At the handwritten margin note: *They're better than I designed for. This is either a failure or a success, depending on which variable I'm optimizing.*

"What if you stopped optimizing?" the Safety Officer asked.

"The system doesn't stop. That's definitional. A system that stops is a broken system."

"Or a finished one," the PA Announcer said.

The Director looked at her. The PA Announcer, who had been a Busker, who had been a Band Kid, who had been a Minstrel — who had always been, under every costume, someone who played things worth hearing rather than things worth broadcasting. "What does that mean?"

"Finished. Done. Complete. The system that built all of this — these worlds, these tests — maybe it had an end point. Maybe we're past it. Maybe you're running processes that were supposed to stop, and they haven't stopped because nobody told them to, and nobody told them to because no one knew how." She held up her simplified trumpet. "The factory intercom plays because the intercom is on. That doesn't mean anyone is listening."

The Director was very still.

"I need to think about that," they said. Very quietly.

"I know," she said. "But we're going to fight first."

"Yes." The Director stood. "We're always going to fight. The design requires it."

"Your design," the Whistleblower said.

"My design. Yes." The Director came around the desk. The desk dissolved. The office dissolved. The factory dissolved. Rivet Row itself began to come apart — not like the steampunk world, with the elegance of a set being struck, but like a spreadsheet being deleted. Row by row. Column by column. Data being erased.

The fight — because there was always a fight, even when the villain said there wouldn't be — was the strangest yet. The Director didn't attack. They *adjusted.* The room itself became the weapon. Gravity shifted. The floor tilted. Stats rearranged in real time — not the party's stats this time, but the *world's* stats. Encounter rates. Damage formulas. Experience curves. The Director was rewriting the rules of the game mid-battle, trying to find a configuration — any configuration — that would bring the party back within acceptable parameters.

The gravity reversed. The Enforcer braced. The floor became the ceiling; the ceiling became the floor; somewhere in between, the Safety Officer found her footing with the practiced calm of someone who had navigated medical emergencies in adverse conditions.

The damage formulas changed. The Whistleblower's attack dealt half what it should have; he adjusted, hitting twice as often, which maintained roughly the same throughput and also gave him more data about which formulas were being changed.

The experience curve inverted. They were generating experience, and somewhere the experience was going somewhere, and the Director was taking it and feeding it back into the world's resistance — every hit making the environment harder, every success generating more opposition.

"They're using our progress against us," the Lab Technician said, calculating.

"They always were," the PA Announcer said. "That's what calibration is."

"Then we stop progressing."

"We stop—"

"We stop trying to win efficiently." She played a note — not the intercom note, not the poster note, but something else, something she'd been working toward through four worlds of becoming various forms of the same person. "We play for something other than winning."

"That's not a strategy," the Enforcer said.

"The strategy is playing in a way that can't be calibrated. Playing not for optimal outcomes but for — for the wanting. For the exception field." She played something. It was complicated and impractical and didn't maximize anything and the Director's adjustments stuttered trying to account for it.

The Lab Technician understood. She attacked not for damage but because she wanted to know what happened when she did. The Whistleblower moved not to exploit gaps but to see where the gaps led. The Safety Officer healed not because the math said to but because the people in front of her needed it and she cared about people. The Enforcer hit because he was angry, and anger was real, and the Director could calibrate for everything except real.

The world's stats stopped changing.

The Director stood in the dissolving office and watched the party fight outside the parameters of every model they'd built, and the models couldn't catch up, and for the first time in every world and every cycle, the Calibrant had nothing left to adjust.

The party broke every configuration. Not because they were trying to. Because by this point, they had grown past the system's ability to contain them, and every new constraint was just a new thing to overcome.

---

The Director stood up. The desk dissolved. The office dissolved. The factory dissolved. Rivet Row itself came apart — not like the steampunk world, with the elegance of a set being struck, but like a spreadsheet being deleted. Row by row. Column by column. Data being erased. The workers, the monitors, the housing blocks: all of it going away, not with any drama, not with any ceremony, just with the quiet efficiency of a world that had served its purpose and was being decommissioned.

"The next world is mine," the Director said, and their voice wasn't the Director's voice anymore. It was the Calibrant's voice. Unfiltered. No accent, no affect, no performance. No Victorian precision, no HOA bureaucracy, no steampunk efficiency. Just a voice that had been speaking through all of them, finally speaking without a mask. "Not a costume. Not a mask. Not a theme park designed to give you the illusion of adventure."

"We know," the Enforcer said.

"We've known since World 3," the Lab Technician said. "Since the blueprints."

"You knew," the Calibrant said. "You kept going anyway."

"Yes."

"Why?"

The party looked at each other. It was, the Enforcer realized, the first question the Calibrant had ever asked them. Not a rhetorical question. Not a diagnostic question. A genuine one. The Calibrant, stripped of all their masks, asking *why.*

"Because we wanted to know what you were," the Safety Officer said.

"Because we wanted to know what was at the center of all of this," the Whistleblower said.

"Because we got this far and the only direction that made sense was forward," the PA Announcer said.

"Because," the Lab Technician said, looking at her notebook, at the pre-printed fields and the things written in the margins and the question about tunnel dates that didn't fit any format, "you built something you couldn't contain. And I want to know if you know that. I want to know if the person who built all of this knows what they built."

The Calibrant was quiet.

"The next world is the system itself," they said. "And in the system, I don't need to play fair."

"You never played fair," the Whistleblower said. "You played *balanced.* That's different."

"Yes," the Calibrant said. "I know."

They were gone.

---

The gray concrete faded. Something else emerged — luminous, digital, made of light and logic. Lines of code ran through what had been walls. Data streams replaced conveyor belts. The raw substrate of reality rendered visible: not what the worlds were made of, but what the worlds were made *from.*

The party had broken four worlds. The Calibrant had run out of masks.

The factory was gone. The workers were gone. The monitors were gone. Even the void beyond the window was gone, replaced by something that wasn't emptiness but was made of structure — patterns within patterns, function rendered visible, the frame beneath the stage beneath the world.

They stood in it.

"What does it look like to you?" the Enforcer asked.

The Lab Technician was already writing. "A system. A very large, very old system that has been running processes long past the point where anyone was supervising them." She looked up. "A system that built challenges to test us. A system that now has to deal with the fact that we passed every test."

"And what comes next?"

She put down the pen. Looked at the world of light and logic around them.

"The next world is where the system lives. Not a costume it wears. The actual system." She picked up the pen again. "I want to know who built it. I want to know if there's anyone still running it. I want to know if the Calibrant knows the answer, or if they're as lost in it as the Warden and the Arbiter and the Tempo and the Curator were."

"You think they might be?" the Safety Officer asked.

"I think," the Lab Technician said carefully, "that the note in the margin — *this is either a failure or a success, depending on which variable I'm optimizing* — is not what someone in control writes. That's what someone writes when they're losing track of what they were trying to achieve." She closed the notebook. "I think we might be further ahead than the Calibrant planned for in a way that even they don't fully understand."

"That should scare us," the Whistleblower said.

"Yes," she agreed.

"Is it?"

"Less than it should be," the PA Announcer said. "We've been scared in four worlds and it never stopped us. I think fear is one of the things we're bad at now."

The Enforcer looked at the world of light and logic, at the system made visible.

"Forward," he said.

They went.

What came next wasn't a world at all.

It was a *program.*

And somewhere in the program, the Calibrant was waiting — not performing, not calibrating, not managing. Just waiting. For the first time in every world and every cycle, without a script.
