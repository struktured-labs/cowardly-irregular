# The Neighborhood Problem

## A Novella of World 2 — Suburban

---

### Prologue: The Wrong Side of the Portal

The first thing they noticed was the smell. Not sulfur, not ozone, not any of the dramatic scents that portals in stories are supposed to produce. It smelled like fresh-cut grass and dryer sheets and something chemical pretending to be lavender.

The second thing they noticed was that they were standing on a sidewalk.

It was a very good sidewalk. Level. Even. Free of cracks, because cracks were a code violation, and code violations were taken seriously in Maple Heights.

A man in cargo shorts and a polo shirt was watering his lawn across the street. He looked at the four armed warriors who had just materialized on his sidewalk. He looked at the sword. The staff. The armor that was, by suburban standards, deeply impractical.

"HOA meeting's not till Thursday," he said, and went back to watering.

---

### Chapter 1: Maple Heights

The suburb existed in a state of aggressive normalcy. Every house was a variation on the same house. Every lawn was the same shade of green, maintained to the same height, edged with the same precision. The sky was blue, but it was the wrong blue — not wrong in a way you could point to, but wrong in a way you could *feel*. Like someone had looked up "sky blue" in a reference manual and matched the hex code exactly without ever having looked at an actual sky.

The party walked down Elm Street (there was also an Oak Street, a Maple Lane, and a Birch Court, because the naming committee had strong feelings about trees and weak feelings about creativity) and tried to understand what they were looking at.

A kid on a bicycle rode past. He slowed down, circled back.

"Cool cosplay," he said. "What convention?"

"We're not—"

"The mall has a Spirit Halloween now. Seasonal, obviously. You could probably get better armor there. No offense."

He rode away. The party stood on the sidewalk and contemplated the concept of seasonal retail.

The strip mall was two blocks east. It had a pizza place, a dry cleaner, a store that sold candles that smelled like other things, and — inexplicably — an armory. Not a sporting goods store. An armory. It was wedged between the candle shop and a frozen yogurt place, and its sign read **YE OLDE MEDIEVAL SURPLUS** in a font that was trying very hard.

Inside, the shelves held items from Harmonia. Not similar items. The *same* items. Potions with the same labels. Swords with the same smithing marks. A shield that one of the party members was fairly certain they had seen on a rack in Ironclad Arms three days and one dimension ago.

The shopkeeper was a teenager in a vest.

"Everything's half off," she said, not looking up from her phone. "We got a huge shipment in from... I don't know, the warehouse? It just shows up."

### Chapter 2: The Rules

The first encounter happened in a cul-de-sac.

A lawn mower — a riding mower, red, the kind that men in the neighborhood discussed with the reverence usually reserved for warhorses — turned itself on. No one was sitting on it. Its blades spun. Its engine growled. It charged.

Behind it, a mailbox opened its door like a mouth. Letters flew out like teeth.

Behind that, a sprinkler system erupted with pressure that shouldn't have been possible from residential plumbing, firing jets of water with the accuracy of a trained archer.

The party fought a lawn mower, a mailbox, and a sprinkler system. They won. No one felt good about it.

"What," said one of them — it didn't matter which one, because they were all thinking it — "is happening."

The answer came from the mail carrier, who had watched the entire fight from behind her truck with the calm of someone who had seen this before.

"It's been like this for months," she said. "Appliances going rogue. Kids disappearing — not missing, just... *relocated.* The HOA says it's 'community restructuring.' Nobody questions it because nobody questions the HOA."

"Why not?"

She looked at them the way you look at someone who has just asked why water is wet.

"Have you *met* the Coordinator?"

---

### Chapter 3: The Coordinator

The Coordinator's office was in the community center, which occupied the exact geographic center of Maple Heights, which was either a coincidence or a statement. The building was beige. The carpeting was beige. The motivational posters on the walls were beige in spirit if not in color, featuring eagles and sunsets and words like COMPLIANCE and COMMUNITY STANDARDS.

They never met the Coordinator in person. Not at first. The Coordinator communicated through memos. Beautifully formatted, grammatically impeccable memos that appeared on every door, in every mailbox, under every windshield wiper in the neighborhood.

**MEMO: RE: UNREGISTERED RESIDENTS**

*It has come to the Community Standards Board's attention that four (4) individuals of unknown origin have taken up residence within the Maple Heights Planned Community without submitting Form 7-A (New Resident Application), Form 12-C (Weapons Declaration), or Form 3-F (Unusual Clothing Variance Request).*

*These individuals are hereby required to report to the Community Center for processing.*

*Sincerely,*
*The Coordinator*
*Maple Heights HOA — "Your Home, Our Standards"*

The retired teacher — Mrs. Chen, who had taught seventh-grade science for thirty years and feared nothing except the HOA's annual review — found them first.

"Don't go to the community center," she said. "People who go to the community center for processing come back... quieter. More cooperative. Like they've been... edited."

"Edited?"

"That's the word that keeps coming to me. I don't know why."

---

### Chapter 4: The Monitor

The neighborhood dad — Greg, who had strong opinions about gas grills and the designated quiet hours — turned out to be more helpful than expected.

"I was on the HOA board," he said, flipping burgers with the intensity of a man who had replaced political anxiety with grilling. "For six months. Before the Coordinator took over."

"What happened?"

"The old board voted to relax the fence height restriction. Two inches. TWO INCHES. The Coordinator called an emergency session. I've never seen anyone take two inches of fence so personally."

He flipped a burger. It sizzled.

"She showed up with data. Charts. Projections. She knew how many code violations each household had committed going back years. She knew our property values down to the dollar. She knew things about the neighborhood that the *neighborhood* didn't know."

He looked at the party. Specifically at the fighter's sword.

"She's going to send compliance officers for you. You know that, right? She doesn't tolerate variance."

The compliance officers were worse than the lawn mowers. They were *people* — neighbors, mostly, wearing matching polos and carrying clipboards that functioned as both documentation tools and surprisingly effective blunt weapons. They didn't fight with malice. They fought with policy. Every strike was accompanied by a citation.

"Violation: unauthorized combat in a residential zone."

*WHACK.*

"Violation: excessive noise after 8 PM."

*WHACK.*

"Violation: failure to maintain a tidy appearance during community engagement."

The party found the Coordinator's private office through the community center's back entrance — past the meeting rooms named after trees (the Elm Room, the Oak Room, the Birch Room — the naming committee had expanded its jurisdiction) and down a hallway that was longer than the building should have allowed.

The office was immaculate. A desk. A lamp. A filing cabinet that contained folders for every resident of Maple Heights, cross-referenced by violation type, compliance score, and something labeled "narrative function."

And on the desk, a monitor.

It was showing World 1. Not a recording — a live view. Stats scrolling in real time. Party composition. Battle history. Autobattle scripts. Total time played. A column labeled "PLAYER BEHAVIOR PROFILE" with entries like "cautious," "aggressive," "systematic," or "chaotic."

At the bottom of the screen, a line of text: **Phase 1 Complete. Phase 2 Active. Calibration: 73%.**

"You're not supposed to see that," said a voice from the doorway.

The Coordinator stood there. She was exactly what the memos suggested — neat, precise, controlled. Middle-aged. Glasses. The kind of person who had never been late for anything in her life, including her own birth.

"But then," she continued, straightening a pen on the desk that was already straight, "you weren't supposed to get this far. The medieval phase usually takes longer."

She said it like she was commenting on a schedule deviation. Not angry. Not threatened. Just... noting the variance.

"Who are you?" the party asked.

"I'm the Coordinator," she said. "I coordinate."

"You're doing more than that."

She smiled. It was the most unsettling smile any of them had ever seen, because it was *genuine.*

"Yes," she said. "I suppose I am."

She fought like a bureaucrat. Forms materialized as barriers. Memos became projectile attacks. The filing cabinet opened and folders flew out, each one containing a record of every mistake the party had ever made — every death, every failed strategy, every wasted item. And each record *hurt*, because knowledge, wielded correctly, is a weapon.

She lost. Of course she lost. That was the point. The party was stronger than the system she'd built, and some part of her had known that before the fight began.

"You're ahead of schedule," she said, as she dissolved. The same way Mordaine had dissolved. Not with drama. With *completion.* "The next phase will require... adjustments."

The monitor on her desk flickered. The Phase 2 display vanished. In its place: a schematic of gears. Brass. Steam. Victorian architecture. A label: **Phase 3: Cogsworth Junction — Calibration Active.**

The office walls began to change. The beige faded. Brass crept in at the edges, like ivy, but metallic. The sound of ticking — faint at first, then undeniable — filled the room.

The suburb didn't end. It *transitioned.* Houses sprouted chimneys that belched steam. Sidewalks buckled and reformed as cobblestone. The sky shifted from wrong-blue to gaslit amber. And in the distance, a clock tower began to chime — not marking the hour, but marking the *change.*

A party member spoke. It didn't matter which one.

"She talked like Mordaine."

Silence.

"Exact same words. 'Order.' 'Purpose.' 'The system.'"

More silence. The kind of silence that happens when something you've been ignoring becomes impossible to ignore.

"They're the same person," someone said. "Aren't they."

No one answered. The clock tower chimed again. The gears turned. The next phase loaded.
