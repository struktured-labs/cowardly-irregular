# The Neighborhood Problem

## A Novella of World 2 — Suburban

---

### Prologue: The Wrong Side of the Portal

The portal didn't do what portals in stories are supposed to do.

There was no flash. No dramatic dimensional tearing. No void-crossing journey with stars streaking past, no cosmic perspective on the smallness of the self. There was a doorframe-shaped shimmer in the air of Harmonia Village, and then there was a step, and then there was sidewalk.

The transformation happened between the shimmer and the step.

The Knight-Errant felt it first — a weight shift, a texture change. The gauntlets were gone. The pauldrons were gone. In their place: something stiff and polyester that smelled of a factory in a country he had no name for. He looked down. Dark trousers with a crease that was too sharp to be natural. A navy jacket with a badge. The badge said: SECURITY.

The sword was not gone. It had become a baton. He held it for a moment — same weight, same balance, same fundamental truth — and tried to decide whether to be concerned about this.

The Friar's robes had become scrubs. Green. With small prints of what might have been angels but were probably more likely cartoon bears. His holy symbol had become a laminated card that read SCHOOL NURSE and his leather medical satchel had become a first-aid kit that had somehow acquired a sticker of a smiling bandage.

He read the card twice. Then he looked at the Knight-Errant's badge. Then he said, "I think we're still supposed to be helping people."

The Hedge Wizard — who had gone through the portal in traveling robes with five pocket-books and a staff that crackled with latent fire — came out the other side in jeans and a t-shirt advertising a university whose name she didn't recognize, carrying a backpack and what appeared to be a scientific calculator. The staff was a pen. Specifically a pen that said PROPERTY OF MAPLE HEIGHTS MIDDLE SCHOOL DO NOT REMOVE, which she had not taken from any middle school, which made the inscription philosophically troubling.

She pressed the pen's button. It clicked. Something in the clicking felt like it still, potentially, contained fire.

The Footpad — Vex — adjusted his hoodie. He had, somehow, acquired a skateboard. He regarded it. He had never in his life stood on a wheeled plank of wood. He stepped on it experimentally and immediately stepped off.

"I know how this works," he said, staring at the board. "I don't know how I know. I just do."

"New world," Mira said, testing the calculator. "New context. Same skills, different costume."

The Minstrel was looking at himself in the reflection of a car window. The lute was gone. In its place: a trumpet case. He opened it. Examined the instrument. Raised it to his lips.

The sound that came out was not exactly music. It was the kind of sound produced by someone who knows in their bones that they are a musician but whose mouth has not yet introduced itself to this particular instrument. A nearby car alarm went off.

"I can work with this," he said, lowering the trumpet.

The man across the street, in cargo shorts and a polo, was watering his lawn. He looked at the five of them with the incurious eyes of a man who has decided that his lawn is his primary concern and everything else is peripheral.

"HOA meeting's not till Thursday," he said, and went back to watering.

### Chapter 1: Maple Heights

The suburb existed in a state of aggressive normalcy. Every house was a variation on the same house. Every lawn was the same shade of green, maintained to the same height, edged with the same precision. The sky was blue, but it was the wrong blue — not wrong in a way you could point to, but wrong in a way you could *feel*. Like someone had looked up "sky blue" in a reference manual and matched the hex code exactly without ever having looked at an actual sky.

The party walked down Elm Street — there was also an Oak Street, a Maple Lane, and a Birch Court, because the naming committee had strong feelings about trees and weak feelings about creativity — and tried to understand what they were looking at.

"It looks like a village," the Knight-Errant said. He was using the word because he didn't have another one.

"It looks like a village that's been through several stages of middle management," Mira said. "Everything is the same distance apart. The same height. The same color within an approved range." She was writing in a new notebook — a spiral-bound one with a cartoon cat on the cover, which had appeared in her backpack and which she was already treating as canonical. "Someone standardized this."

"Someone standardized the trees," the Friar said, looking at the evenly spaced oaks lining the street. "Those are the same species, the same age, the same pruning. That's not natural growth."

"Nothing here is natural growth," Vex said.

A kid on a bicycle rode past. He slowed down, circled back, and examined them with the open assessment of someone who has not yet learned to pretend they aren't staring.

"Cool cosplay," he said. "What convention?"

"We're not—"

"The mall has a Spirit Halloween now. Seasonal, obviously. You could probably get better armor there. No offense." He looked at the baton on the security guard's hip. "Or, like, the party store for the cop stuff. They have those."

He rode away. The party stood on the sidewalk and contemplated the concept of seasonal retail.

"He called this armor," the Knight-Errant said, looking at his jacket.

"He called it *cosplay,*" Mira said. "Which means he thinks we're dressed as something." She looked down at her own outfit. "Dressed as people who belong in a different... genre."

"Dressed as ourselves in a different world," the Minstrel said. He had opened the trumpet case again and was touching the instrument with the tentative affection of someone bonding with livestock they hadn't chosen. "That's either profound or embarrassing and I haven't decided which."

The strip mall was two blocks east. It had a pizza place, a dry cleaner, a store that sold candles that smelled like other things, and — inexplicably — an armory. Not a sporting goods store. An armory. It was wedged between the candle shop and a frozen yogurt place, and its sign read **YE OLDE MEDIEVAL SURPLUS** in a font that was trying very hard.

Inside, the shelves held items from Harmonia. Not similar items. The *same* items. Potions with the same labels. Swords with the same smithing marks. A shield that one of the party members was fairly certain they had seen on a rack in Ironclad Arms three days and one dimension ago.

The shopkeeper was a teenager in a vest who was doing something on her phone that required more attention than anything in the physical world.

"Everything's half off," she said, not looking up. "We got a huge shipment in from... I don't know, the warehouse? It just shows up."

"From Harmonia?" the Friar asked.

"From wherever. I just ring them up." She tapped her screen. "You want the potions? They're the only things that actually work. The weapons are decorative, which I am legally required to say. The potions are just really good juice or whatever."

"They are very much not juice," Vex said.

"Right, yeah. Wink wink." She finally looked up, assessed the group, looked back at her phone. "You're from the other side of the portal, aren't you. Don't worry about it. People come through sometimes. You get used to it."

"Who else has come through?" Mira asked.

"Mostly just you guys, this cycle." She scrolled. "Before that, four other people. Before that, a guy on his own who freaked out and went back immediately." She shrugged. "The HOA usually catches them before they cause variance."

"Variance?"

She finally set the phone down. "Honey," she said, and her voice had the tired patience of a seventeen-year-old who has been dealing with this for longer than seventeen-year-olds should have to deal with anything, "you are five people in costumes carrying medieval weaponry in a suburb where the mailboxes are *already* alive. You are maximum variance. The Coordinator is going to find you within the day. Stock up first."

### Chapter 2: The Rules

The first encounter happened in a cul-de-sac three streets from the strip mall.

A lawn mower — a riding mower, red, the kind that men in the neighborhood discussed with the reverence usually reserved for warhorses — sat in someone's driveway until the party walked past it. Then it sat in the driveway differently. The blades, which had been still, began to spin with a sound like intent rather than mechanism. The engine coughed to life. The seat, empty, creaked as if taking weight.

The mower reversed out of the driveway.

"Oh no," said the Minstrel, who had the most active relationship with hope and therefore the most to lose.

Behind the mower, a mailbox opened its door with a creak that was too slow and too deliberate to be wind. Letters flew out — envelopes and flyers and what appeared to be a community newsletter — in a constellation pattern that suggested aerodynamic intention rather than postal chaos.

Behind that, a sprinkler system erupted from the lawn with pressure that shouldn't have been possible from residential plumbing, firing jets of water in tight, precise arcs.

"The lawn mower is the tank," Mira said, backing up and pulling out her calculator. "High defense. Low mobility. Focus magical damage — fire pen, if the mower is combustible." She pressed a button on the calculator. "The mailbox has ranged attack and paper-cut damage, which sounds minor but—"

An envelope hit Vex in the ear with enough force to ring it. He made a sound of offense.

"—is not minor. Neutralize the mailbox first. The sprinkler has area control. We need to move constantly or it—"

The sprinkler tracked directly to the Knight-Errant's position and hit him dead center in the chest.

"—locks on," Mira finished. "It has targeting. Somehow."

The fight that followed was undignified in ways that the party had not, in their combined experience, encountered. Defeating a riding lawn mower with a security baton required a commitment to not thinking too hard about what you were doing. The Friar's healing hands, applied to ears stinging from paper cuts, felt like a misallocation of divine grace. The Hedge Wizard's fire — drawn from the pen, which did in fact contain fire, though less of it than the staff had managed — turned out to be rather more effective against a gas-powered engine than court magic had ever been against a stone golem, and the mower died with a spectacular exhaust backfire that set the Minstrel's pants briefly alight.

They won. No one felt good about it.

"What," said the Knight-Errant — it didn't matter which of them said it first, because they were all thinking it — "is happening."

The mail carrier, who had watched the entire fight from behind her truck with the calm of someone who had seen this before, pulled off her headphones.

"It's been like this for months," she said. "Appliances going rogue. Kids disappearing — not missing, just... *relocated.* The HOA says it's 'community restructuring.' Nobody questions it because nobody questions the HOA."

"Why not?"

She looked at them the way you look at someone who has just asked why water is wet.

"Have you *met* the Coordinator?"

### Chapter 3: The Warden of Routine

They met the first Masterite at the crosswalk on Birch Court and Cedar Avenue.

She was wearing an orange vest and carrying a STOP sign — the paddle kind, on a pole, that crossing guards use. She was standing in the exact center of the crosswalk, and she had clearly been standing there for some time, because the paint of the walk had worn around her feet in the pattern of long occupancy.

She held up the sign as they approached.

"Halt," she said.

"We need to cross the street," the Knight-Errant said.

"No crossing without the signal," the Warden of Routine said. She did not move. Her expression was the expression of someone who has found, in the management of a single intersection, the full expression of their purpose. "The signal says WALK. I say WALK. Not before. Not after. Not due to extenuating circumstances."

"There's no traffic," Mira said, looking both ways at the completely empty street.

"The absence of visible traffic does not indicate the absence of traffic." The Warden turned the sign toward them. STOP. "This intersection has been under my supervision since the beginning of the neighborhood. I have never had an incident. I intend to maintain that record."

"We'll be quick," Vex said.

"Crossing without the signal is a violation. Violations are not quick. Violations have consequences. Consequences take time." Her grip on the pole tightened. "Stand behind the line."

Mira looked at the ground. There was no painted line.

"There's no line."

"The line is implied," the Warden said. "Resident knowledge of implied lines is part of community standards compliance."

"We're not residents."

"You're standing in my intersection. That makes you my responsibility." She raised the stop sign slightly. "And you are out of compliance."

The fight was disorienting in the way that only truly committed bureaucratic obstruction can be. The Warden hit with the stop sign — which was, it turned out, enchanted in the same way the Warden of the Old Guard's shield had been, with the same kind of structured magic that had no right being present in a road safety implement. She placed traffic cones with a precision that constrained the party's movement. She deployed speed bumps from her vest pockets that appeared on the battlefield floor and caused genuine tactical problems.

She was difficult in the way a truly well-designed obstacle is difficult. Not unfair. Just: committed to her purpose beyond any reasonable interpretation of that purpose.

When she finally went down — when the Friar's persistence and the Hedge Wizard's creative application of electrical interference (traffic signals ran on circuits; the pen, as it turned out, also ran on circuits, after a fashion) broke through her endurance — she set her stop sign carefully against a lamppost and sat on the curb.

"You may cross," she said.

"Thank you," the Friar said.

"You're welcome." She looked at the empty street. "I've been at this crosswalk for... how long?" She frowned. "It feels like longer than the neighborhood's been here."

"How long has the neighborhood been here?" Mira asked.

"Since the beginning." The Warden looked up at them. "That's what I was told. This neighborhood has always been here. I have always been at this intersection." She paused. "Except I can't remember the first day. I can't remember being assigned here. I just... was. And then I was here." She picked up the sign again with the automatic motion of someone who needs to be doing their job or the ground feels uncertain. "You should cross now. Before traffic comes."

There was, as there had been throughout the conversation, no traffic.

They crossed.

### Chapter 4: Garage Sales and Other Hazards

The garage sale on Oak Street had a sign: EVERYTHING MUST GO. The sign was large and bright and clearly visible from both directions, which was why the party stopped.

They should not have stopped.

The tables were covered with the detritus of suburban accumulation — ceramic figurines, folded sweaters, stacks of VHS tapes, a bread maker with the hopeful air of something that had been used twice and kept in case of a second chance. Behind the tables, an older woman in a floral dress sat in a lawn chair reading a paperback, the entirety of her garage laid out for the neighborhood's consideration.

"Half off everything," she said, not looking up. "Cash only. No returns."

The Friar, who had the clearest understanding of the party's financial situation in this world and had discovered that a nurse's salary did not, in fact, exist in their cash-on-hand, began examining the tables for anything useful.

He picked up a sweater.

The sweater resisted.

Not with the passive resistance of a sweater that has tangled on something. With the active resistance of a sweater that has decided it does not want to leave. It pulled back. He pulled forward. It escalated. Within three seconds, three additional items had joined the conflict: a ceramic rooster, a set of mixing bowls stacked in a tower, and a garden gnome that had somehow repositioned itself from the far end of the table to directly in front of Vex, who had been thinking about the garden gnome and whether its slightly threatening smile was intentional design or unfortunate craftsmanship.

"I probably shouldn't have touched the sweater," the Friar said, still holding his end of it.

"No one told us about the sweater," Vex said, taking a step back from the gnome.

"The sign says everything must go," the older woman said, turning a page. "It doesn't say it has to be *easy.*"

The garden gnome lunged.

What followed was a full engagement with four enchanted garage sale items across a cluttered suburban driveway, which required more spatial awareness than any of the cave fights had, because the terrain was tables and price-tagged knickknacks and a bread maker that had apparently decided this was its moment. The ceramic rooster's pecking attack was technically weaker than most cave goblins but it was ceramic and therefore could not be persuaded to stop by being spoken to. The mixing bowls operated in formation. The gnome was fast in the specific way of things that should not be fast, which is to say startlingly.

They managed it — the Knight-Errant's baton was well-suited to the gnome situation, the Hedge Wizard's electrical pen had interesting effects on ceramic, and Vex solved the mixing bowl formation problem by the simple expedient of stacking them in a different order, which seemed to break the enchantment.

The sweater, finally, let go.

The older woman had not looked up during any of this.

"You can keep the sweater," she said. "You earned it."

The Friar examined the sweater. It was warm. It had a small embroidered bear on the pocket. It was, all things considered, quite nice.

He kept it.

### Chapter 5: The Arbiter of the Grade

The school sat at the end of Maple Lane with the weight of a building that knows it is important. MAPLE HEIGHTS ELEMENTARY, read the sign out front, and below that, in smaller letters: EXCELLENCE THROUGH ACCOUNTABILITY.

They had been inside for approximately forty seconds when the loudspeaker crackled.

"Good morning, unregistered visitors. Please proceed directly to Room 204 for assessment. Refusal to proceed constitutes a violation of school policy and will be noted in your permanent record."

"We don't have permanent records," Vex said.

"*Yet,*" said the loudspeaker, and clicked off.

Room 204 had the ambient temperature of a place where stress lived and raised a family. The desks were standard school desks with the attached flip-up writing surface, arranged in perfect rows. At the front stood a man in a dress shirt and tie who had the bearing of someone who considered a three-point rubric to be a statement of personal philosophy.

The Arbiter of the Grade looked at the party and began distributing papers.

"Pop quiz," he said.

"We're not students," the Knight-Errant said.

"This school serves the community," the Arbiter said. "You are in the community. You are being served." He set the papers down with precision — one in front of each party member. "You have eight minutes."

Mira picked up her paper. The first question read: *Identify the underlying economic conditions that led to the decline of the merchant class in the transitional period between feudal and proto-capitalist systems of resource distribution. Show all work.*

"That's a graduate-level economics question," she said.

"The rubric rewards partial credit," the Arbiter said.

Vex's first question: *Your skateboard travels at an average speed of 12 kilometers per hour. You are being pursued by a compliance officer at 15 kilometers per hour. The officer began pursuit 200 meters behind you. How long until you are apprehended, and is this outcome avoidable?*

"This one's personal," Vex said.

"All learning is personal," the Arbiter said. "Pencils up."

The quiz attacks were, in practice, each wrong answer manifesting as direct damage — specific, targeted, and scaled to the magnitude of the incorrectness rather than simply the occurrence of an error. The correct answers reduced the damage but did not eliminate it entirely, because, as the Arbiter noted, the learning curve was the point.

The Minstrel answered every question in verse and received partial credit on all of them, which was the highest partial credit the Arbiter had ever awarded to a non-standard response format.

The Friar answered his questions ("Define the moral calculus of triage in a resource-limited medical setting, citing at least two ethical frameworks") with the genuine thoughtfulness of someone who had actually practiced triage and found the academic framework less useful than the lived version. The Arbiter marked two of his answers incorrect due to "insufficient citation" but acknowledged, in a margin note, that the answers were correct in practice.

The fight broke out when the Knight-Errant answered the question about the chain of command in a feudal military hierarchy by explaining, with polite earnestness, that in his experience the chain of command ran directly to whoever was willing to do the work, and the Arbiter declared this ideologically out of standard and assigned a zero.

"You cannot assign me a zero," the Knight-Errant said. "I answered correctly."

"The answer was not within the established parameters," the Arbiter said.

"The parameters are wrong."

"The parameters are the rubric," the Arbiter said, and the rubric appeared as a physical document, and the physical document became a weapon in a way that felt like either metaphor or justice depending on your perspective.

The grading battle was close. The Arbiter's academic precision translated into a fighting style of calibrated, optimal strikes — the same principle as the medieval Arbiter, rephrased for a setting where the vocabulary of excellence was measured in percentages. It adapted. When the party found a strategy that worked, it revised its approach with the unhurried confidence of someone who has been revising their approach for longer than the party had been fighting together.

They passed. Technically. The Friar's rubric-compliant approach and the Hedge Wizard's technically correct answers on the science and mathematics questions accumulated enough points to meet the curve.

The Arbiter received this outcome with the equanimity of someone who finds partial victories informative.

"My rubric was perfect," he said, sitting on the edge of his desk. "My curve was calibrated precisely. How did you pass without studying?"

"We studied our whole lives," Mira said. "Just not these subjects."

"The underlying principles are the same," the Friar added. "Every healing decision uses triage logic. Every tactical choice is resource allocation."

The Arbiter looked at his grade book. "You are not the students this curriculum was designed for," he said. "And yet the curriculum worked." A pause. "Partially. With significant deviation." He closed the grade book. "I don't know whether to mark that as a success."

"Mark it as interesting," the Minstrel suggested.

"I don't have a column for interesting," the Arbiter said. But he wrote something in the margin anyway.

### Chapter 6: The Tempo of the Rush Hour

They heard the car before they saw it.

It was the kind of sound a car makes when it has been asked to operate at a speed that implies a philosophical commitment to arriving. The engine had clearly been modified. The suspension had clearly been abused. The car itself — a beat-up compact with a magnetic sign on the roof reading MAPLE HEIGHTS PIZZA — came around the corner of Birch Court at a speed that was ambitious for a residential zone.

The window rolled down. A head appeared.

"THIRTY MINUTES OR LESS!" shouted the Tempo of the Rush Hour.

"Thirty minutes or less what?" the Friar said.

The answer was the car accelerating toward them.

The chase through Maple Heights' subdivisions was a different kind of test than the forest run had been. Scriptura's alleys had been navigable, knowable. The suburb's cul-de-sacs and through-streets and sudden dead ends were designed with the logic of a place that expected its residents to have GPS and twenty years of familiarity. The party had neither. The Tempo had both, and the car, and a genuine professional commitment to completing the delivery.

Vex discovered, around the second lap of the subdivision, that the skateboard worked the way it was supposed to work — which is to say that it translated his existing spatial instincts into a different vocabulary. He could outmaneuver a car in places a car couldn't fit. He did. He led the Tempo down a footpath between houses that ended in a yard with a fence.

The car hit the fence. The fence won, temporarily.

In the moment of the Tempo's extraction from the fence situation — car in reverse, backing onto the street, engine screaming — the party assembled.

The fight was fast and loud and municipal-scale wrong. The Tempo used the car itself as a weapon of crowd control, and the Knight-Errant's baton against the chassis had the same structural logic as sword against armor but with worse optics. The Hedge Wizard's electrical pen had excellent synergy with a car engine, which she noted with academic satisfaction. The Minstrel played the trumpet during the fight, which either boosted the party or confused the Tempo or both; the causality was unclear and she didn't stop.

When the car finally sputtered out — electrical system compromised, fence-damage compounded — the Tempo stepped out, brushing broken glass from the pizza company's branded windbreaker.

"Heck of a chase," the Tempo said, with genuine appreciation. "I've been doing this route my whole life." They looked at the car. Back at the party. "Except..." Something shifted in the easy confidence of the expression. "I don't remember starting. I've been delivering to this neighborhood my whole life, and I remember every house, every regular order, every dog that runs at the car, but I don't remember the first delivery." They looked at their own hands. "That's weird, right? That should be weird."

"It's okay," the Friar said.

"It's *not* okay," the Tempo said, not with anger but with the specific distress of someone accessing a feeling they weren't prepared to have. "A person remembers their first job. A person remembers the beginning." They looked up. "How long have you been doing yours?"

"We've been doing ours," the Minstrel said carefully, "since a notice was nailed to a door."

"That's specific." The Tempo looked at the party with new attention. "That's a beginning. That's a real one." Something moved behind their eyes. "I want one of those. I should have one of those."

They drove away, slowly, one wheel bent, and the party stood in a cul-de-sac at the end of Birch Court and watched them go.

### Chapter 7: Feral Infrastructure

The parking lot of the strip mall had, in the weeks since the party arrived, been developing a reputation.

Specifically: a shopping cart.

Not a fleet of shopping carts. One. The others were docked in their return stations, compliant, corralled. This one had escaped its corral at some point and had been ranging freely through the parking lot, operating on a logic that no one had yet reverse-engineered. It did not move when observed. It moved when you turned your back. It had, according to the teenager at Ye Olde Medieval Surplus, been "coming in through the back door" after hours, and she had stopped questioning it because there were larger problems.

The party encountered the feral shopping cart while trying to take a shortcut through the parking lot to the community center.

It was waiting.

Not ambushing, precisely. More: positioned. Positioned in the way that the Warden had been positioned in the cave — with the deliberate patience of something that has taken a job seriously and is ready to do it. The cart was blocking the most direct path.

"Are we going to fight a shopping cart," Vex said.

"We fought a lawn mower," the Friar said.

"The lawn mower had context. The mower was protecting the lawn. This cart is—"

The cart charged.

Shopping carts are not aerodynamically designed for speed, but this one had apparently solved that problem through a combination of resolve and the slight downward grade of the parking lot. It hit the Knight-Errant's shins with authority and then spun, which carts do, but this spin was controlled, a defensive rotation that put the sharp corner toward the next attacker.

"It fights like a Tempo," Mira said. "Speed-based. Uses geometry."

"It's a *shopping cart,*" Vex said.

"It's a very good shopping cart," the Minstrel said, backing away from it.

They drove it into the cart return. This required everyone working in concert, because the cart didn't want to go, and there was a dignity-versus-effectiveness tension in the project of physically herding an enchanted shopping cart into a designated return space. But once it was there — once it was *corralled* — something went out of it. The enchantment, or whatever it was, dissipated. The wheels locked. The cart became, again, just a cart.

"I feel bad," the Friar said.

"Don't feel bad about the cart," Vex said.

"It was just doing what carts do," the Friar said. "It was just doing it with more commitment than expected."

"We put it back where it belongs," the Knight-Errant said. "That's not a cruelty."

Nobody said anything. It was a parking lot. They moved on.

*I must note for the record that the garage sale enchantments at 14 Oak Street were performing at expected parameters until the male subject in the nurse's uniform picked up the beige sweater and chose to keep it. The sweater was programmed to deter. He kept it anyway. He is wearing it.*

*This was not a designed outcome. The sweater is not meaningful. And yet.*

*He is wearing it in a way that suggests it is.*

*I am noting this because I track all deviations from expected subject behavior and this is a deviation. I am not noting this because I have any particular feeling about it. I am simply thorough.*

*The subject in the nurse's uniform has a high degree of what my old metrics called "faith investment" and what I am revising my metrics to call "quality of attention." He attends to things — the old king, the Warden's lesson, the sweater — in a way that my models struggle to account for.*

*I do not know what to do with "quality of attention" as a metric.*

*I am flagging this as a gap in my assessment framework. The Arbiter flagged the same thing. We are converging on the same gap from different directions. This is either a coincidence or it is not.*

*— The Coordinator*

### Chapter 9: The Curator of the Budget

The accountant had an office in the strip mall, between the frozen yogurt place and the candle shop. The door had a handwritten sign: BUDGET SOLUTIONS — WALK-INS WELCOME. The sign was a lie, but the door was unlocked.

Inside: a desk covered in ledgers. A man in a short-sleeved dress shirt and a tie that had been perfect at the start of the day. A filing cabinet that occupied far more wall than the room's geometry should have allowed. The smell of toner and anxiety.

The Curator of the Budget looked up from his spreadsheets.

"Ah," he said. "The irregulars."

"You know who we are," Mira said.

"I know your financial profile," he said. "Fourteen potions consumed over the residential phase. Two swords — one with irregular provenance, one repurposed from a medieval baton-equivalent. Total GP equivalent spent at Ye Olde Medieval Surplus: seventy-three dollars and forty cents." He turned the ledger toward them. "You're not from here. Your itemized deductions are unusual." He tapped the page. "Medieval healing potions. Those aren't in any tax bracket I recognize."

"We've been spending across multiple world states," Mira said, because this was, technically, the accurate description.

"Multi-jurisdictional deductions are complicated," the Curator said. He produced a form. It was IRS Form 4797. It had been modified to include a field labeled "DIMENSIONAL ORIGIN" and another labeled "RESOURCE STATE AT WORLD ENTRY." "I've taken the liberty of beginning your assessment. I'll need the primary taxpayer to sit down."

"We're not filing taxes," Vex said.

"You have resources," the Curator said. "Resources imply taxation. Taxation implies filing." He opened his briefcase. Inside: a clipboard, a calculator that was much better than Mira's, and what appeared to be a weapon made of laminated forms. "I've been very patient. The HOA has been very patient. The system requires that you file."

The fight with the Curator of the Budget was a sustained drain — not on health, but on every other resource the party had accumulated. The laminated forms could strip potions from inventory. The audit conducted mid-battle identified every buff the party was running and filed for an "itemized deduction" against each one. The briefcase contained a secondary filing that appeared to function as a debuff cloud. At one point, the calculator produced a number that was described, mechanically, as "your remaining resource potential after taxes," and the number was not zero but it was low.

"You are running at a net negative," the Curator said, halfway through. He was not gloating. He was reporting. "Current asset-to-liability ratio suggests you cannot sustain this engagement."

"Then we'd better end it," the Hedge Wizard said, and stopped holding back her electrical pen.

The pen had been, throughout the suburban phase, operating at somewhere between sixty and seventy percent capacity. The fire was there. The electricity was there. The magic was there. But the world's genre had imposed friction — the same way the sword had become a baton, the staff had become a pen, and something about *pen* said *careful,* said *precise,* said *measured.*

The Hedge Wizard decided to stop being measured.

The Curator's filing cabinet opened during the burst, and the files inside flew — and they were, it turned out, a mistake. Not malicious. Not strategic. Each file was a record: purchase histories, HOA violation reports, and something else, something at the back, something labeled with the party's names and the heading "PHASE 2 SUBJECT PROFILES — FORWARDED TO COORDINATOR."

They had three seconds to read before the files dispersed.

What Mira read in those three seconds, she wrote down later, in the spiral notebook with the cartoon cat cover.

The Curator received his defeat with the silence of someone who has received bad news from a ledger before and has developed equanimity through practice.

"Your itemized deductions," he said, as the files settled, "are unlike anything in the system." He looked at his own hands — at the forms, the clipboard, the briefcase. "And my system is comprehensive. My records here cover every resident since the neighborhood was established." He looked up. "I have records for residents who do not remember living here. I have records for people who don't exist in any other database." A pause. "That should bother me more than it does."

"Does it bother you at all?" the Friar asked.

"Yes," the Curator said, after a moment. "A little. More today than yesterday." He closed the briefcase. "That's new."

### Chapter 10: The Monitor

The community center occupied the exact geographic center of Maple Heights, which was either a coincidence or a statement.

They found the back entrance through the Oak Room — one of the meeting rooms named after trees, which the Footpad had identified during the third day of their residence as the most navigable part of the building from a structural standpoint. The hallway beyond the meeting rooms was longer than the building should have allowed, which was the kind of spatial inconsistency that had stopped startling them roughly around the third day.

The Coordinator's private office was at the end.

It was immaculate. A desk. A lamp. A filing cabinet that contained folders for every resident of Maple Heights, cross-referenced by violation type, compliance score, and something labeled "narrative function." The folders for the party were the thickest. Their labels read: UNREGISTERED. STATUS: ACTIVE.

And on the desk, a monitor.

It was showing World 1. Not a recording — a live view. Stats scrolling in real time. Party composition. Battle history. Autobattle scripts, if they'd used them. Total time played. A column labeled "PLAYER BEHAVIOR PROFILE" with entries like "cautious," "aggressive," "systematic," "irregular." A column labeled "MASTERITE FEEDBACK: PHASE 1" with four rows, each populated.

The Warden's row read: *Adaptive. Corrects in-fight. Responds to mentorship. Unusually receptive.*

The Arbiter's row read: *Irregular formation. Fights on principles rather than tactics. Effective.*

The Tempo's row read: *Spatial intelligence. Decision-making under pressure. Memory-related observation: they have a beginning.*

The Curator's row: *Resource management: unconventional but effective. Primary mage documents everything. Note.*

At the bottom of the screen, a line of text: **Phase 1 Complete. Phase 2 Active. Calibration: 73%.**

"You're not supposed to see that," said a voice from the doorway.

The Coordinator stood there. She was exactly what the memos had suggested — neat, precise, controlled. Middle-aged. Glasses. The kind of person who had never been late for anything in her life, including her own birth. She wore the expression of someone who has found a variance in their system and is assessing whether it is a problem or data.

"But then," she continued, straightening a pen on the desk that was already straight, "you weren't supposed to get this far. The medieval phase usually takes longer."

She said it like she was commenting on a schedule deviation. Not angry. Not threatened. Just noting the variance.

"Who are you?" the party asked.

"I'm the Coordinator," she said. "I coordinate."

"You're doing more than that."

She smiled. It was the most unsettling smile any of them had ever seen, because it was *genuine.*

"Yes," she said. "I suppose I am."

"Mordaine," the Minstrel said. He had his notebook open. He had been writing since the filing cabinet. "Chancellor Mordaine said: 'The kingdom needed order. You needed a challenge. Both served their purpose.' She said: 'You were the variable I introduced to see if the system could adapt.'" He looked up. "You're going to say something like that."

A pause. The Coordinator's expression did not change.

"The neighborhood needed rules," she said. "You needed resistance. Both served their purpose."

The Minstrel closed his notebook.

"Yes," he said. "That's it."

The fight was bureaucratic in the same way that all fights with Mordaine's masks were bureaucratic — the Coordinator fought with the tools of administration, elevated. Forms materialized as barriers. Memos became projectile attacks. The filing cabinet opened and folders flew out, each one containing a record of every mistake the party had ever made — every suboptimal decision, every failed strategy, every wasted resource. And each record *hurt*, because documentation, wielded correctly, is leverage.

But where Mordaine had manipulated the battlefield physically, the Coordinator manipulated *context*. When the Knight-Errant swung his baton, a form appeared mid-swing: LIABILITY WAIVER — PLEASE SIGN BEFORE PROCEEDING WITH PHYSICAL ENGAGEMENT. He didn't sign it. The form cited him for the unsigned form. The citation accumulated to a fine. The fine extracted gold from the party's inventory.

"You're weaponizing paperwork," Mira said, blocking a memo with her notebook.

"I'm streamlining accountability," the Coordinator said.

They won. The same way they had won against Mordaine — not through optimal strategy, because optimal strategy was something the Coordinator could predict and counter. Through the accumulated specific knowledge of who they had become since Harmonia.

The Friar healed beyond reasonable resource parameters because he had made a habit of keeping something in reserve for the moment when things looked impossible.

The Hedge Wizard stopped being measured.

Vex found angles that the Coordinator's spatial models didn't account for, because her models were built on a neighborhood she understood and Vex had spent his entire career exploiting the assumption of the predictable.

The Knight-Errant, when a liability form appeared mid-swing, reached into his jacket with his off hand and produced a pen — the Minstrel's extra pen, borrowed before the community center visit — and signed it without stopping the swing. The form dissolved. The fine didn't apply.

"You cannot both sign and contest," the Coordinator said, briefly wrong-footed.

"I signed," he said. "I didn't contest. I just continued." He met her eyes. "There's no form for that."

She lost. Of course she lost. That was the point. And when she dissolved — the same way Mordaine had dissolved, the same absence rather than an ending — her final words carried the same vocabulary, the same philosophy, the same double meaning.

"You're ahead of schedule," she said. "The next phase will require... adjustments."

### Chapter 11: After

The monitor on her desk flickered. The Phase 2 display vanished. In its place: a schematic of gears. Brass. Steam. Victorian architecture. A label: **Phase 3: Cogsworth Junction — Calibration Active.**

The party stood in the Coordinator's office with the files still scattered and the sense of something ending that was not quite a victory.

"She talked like Mordaine," the Minstrel said.

"Exact same words," Mira said. "'Order.' 'Purpose.' 'The system.'"

"They're the same person," someone said.

"Not a person," Mira said. "Something that wears people. Wears the right person for the world it's built."

"It built this," the Knight-Errant said. He was looking at the files — all those folders, all those residents, all those narratives. "All of this. The neighborhood. The Masterites. The memos."

"The challenge," the Minstrel said. He had the notebook out again. "It keeps building challenges." He looked around the immaculate office, the wrong-blue sky outside the window. "We beat Mordaine and it built the Coordinator. We beat the Coordinator and something else is already building in Cogsworth Junction." He wrote something. "It's not a villain. It's a *system.* A system that generates villains to test us."

"For what purpose?" the Friar asked.

"That's the question," Mira said.

"It calibrates," Vex said. "That's what it keeps doing. The cave was calibrated. The forest was calibrated. The neighborhood was calibrated." He picked up one of the files — a resident's compliance history, years of it, meticulously documented. "All of this is just measurement. Every encounter, every Masterite, every lawn mower. It's measuring something about us."

"What?" the Friar asked.

Nobody answered. The monitor showed gears turning. The office walls began to change — the beige fading, brass creeping in at the edges, the smell of oil and coal smoke threading through the lavender candle scent.

"The Masterites," the Knight-Errant said quietly. "The Warden asked if we'd earned the right to rest. The Arbiter asked where we learned to fight. The Tempo asked about our beginning." He looked at the party. "The Curator's last words were: 'That's new. More today than yesterday.'" He paused. "They're waking up."

"Or noticing," Mira said. "Same thing."

"Does the system know?" Vex asked.

"The memos suggest it's noticing but not understanding it," Mira said. "She flagged it as off-profile." She looked at the last memo on the desk — the one that had been in progress when they arrived, half-written, the pen still sitting across it. She read the last line, which the Coordinator had not yet finished.

*The subjects demonstrate something my models have no category for. It is not tactical excellence. It is not resource optimization. It is —*

The sentence ended there. The pen had been set down. The Coordinator had been going to the door.

Mira read the incomplete sentence twice.

"She was going to write something she'd never written before," the Minstrel said, reading over her shoulder.

"She didn't get to finish."

"No."

The clock tower — newly present, newly real, rising through the community center's roof like a brass shoot from old soil — began to chime. Not marking the hour. Marking the change.

The suburb didn't end. It *transitioned.* Houses sprouted chimneys that belched steam. Sidewalks buckled and reformed as cobblestone. The sky shifted from wrong-blue to gaslit amber. And in the distance, gears turned with the sound of something that had been running for a very long time, unobserved, and had decided it was time to be visible.

"Phase Three," the Minstrel said.

"Cogsworth Junction," Mira confirmed.

"A steampunk city," the Knight-Errant said.

"Built by the same something," Vex said. "Managed by the same something. With the same something waiting at the top."

"Except this time," the Minstrel said, "we know to look."

The office walls completed their transformation. The filing cabinets became brass-fitted cabinets with combination locks. The compliance posters became technical schematics. The motivational eagles became gears with latin mottoes about precision and tolerance.

The party stood in a room that had been one thing and was becoming another, and they were the only constant in it, and that felt, for the first time, like it might be the point.

"She was going to write something," Mira said again, looking at the incomplete sentence. "'It is not tactical excellence. It is not resource optimization. It is—'"

"What?" the Knight-Errant asked.

"I don't know," she said. "But I think it's what she's been building all of this to figure out." She closed her notebook. "We should go find out."

The clock tower chimed again. The gears turned. The next phase loaded.
