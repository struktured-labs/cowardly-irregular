# The Regulator

## A Novella of World 3 — Steampunk

---

### Prologue: Brass

The gears were already turning when they arrived. Not starting — *already turning*. As though the world had been running before they got there and would continue running after they left, and their presence was, at best, a scheduled event.

Cogsworth Junction smelled like oil and copper and the particular kind of ambition that builds things too large to be practical and then insists they are essential. The sky was the color of old pennies. Every surface was brass or iron or some alloy between the two that no one had bothered to name because naming it would have taken time away from polishing it.

A clock tower stood at the center of everything. It was too tall. Not architecturally — structurally, it was sound. It was too tall *philosophically*. It was the kind of tower that existed to remind you that time was passing and that someone was keeping track.

It chimed as they arrived. Thirteen times. There are no thirteen o'clocks. They were being announced.

### Chapter 1: Clockwork

Brasston was a market town that ran like a pocket watch.

This was not a metaphor. The citizens moved on tracks — not literal tracks, but paths so ingrained, so repeated, so perfectly timed that they might as well have been grooves cut into the cobblestone. The baker opened at six. The smithy at seven. The lamplighter began his rounds at dusk, and dusk happened at the same minute every day because the gas lamps were also the sky, and the sky was also a mechanism.

Sprocket found them within four minutes of arrival. He knew this because he checked his watch.

"Newcomers! Welcome to Brasston! You're—" he checked his watch again — "seven seconds late. My fault, I should have accounted for dimensional transition lag."

He said this like it was a normal thing to account for.

"Everything here runs on the Grand Schedule," Sprocket explained, walking them through streets where every citizen nodded at exactly the right moment, as if their greetings were synchronized to a master clock. "The Regulator set it up years ago. Or maybe always. Nobody remembers a time before the schedule, which is either impressive institutional memory or suspicious, depending on your outlook."

"Who is the Regulator?" the party asked, though by now they were learning to dread the answer to that question.

"Chief Engineer of the Grand Mechanism. Lives in the central works. Keeps everything running. Nobody sees them much, but you can *feel* them. In the way the gears turn. In the way the trains arrive. In the way—"

"—everything is orderly and controlled and exactly where it's supposed to be?"

Sprocket blinked. "You've met someone like the Regulator before?"

"Twice."

Clem the Lamplighter found them at dusk, which was scheduled for 6:47 PM and arrived at 6:47 PM with the precision of a guillotine blade.

"I work the night shift," he said, lighting a lamp that ignited before his match reached it, because the lamp was on a timer. "Most folk don't notice me. But I notice things."

"Things in the pipes," the party said. They'd read ahead.

Clem stared at them. "How did you—"

"Lucky guess."

"The things in the pipes aren't things," Clem said, lowering his voice, though in Brasston, lowering your voice was a schedule deviation and would normally trigger a warning. "They're *signals*. Instructions. The pipes don't just carry steam. They carry... orders. From the Mechanism. To everything."

He looked up at the clock tower.

"And the orders have been changing lately. Getting... tighter. More specific. Like whoever's giving them is worried about something. Or some*one*."

Madame Orrery's shop was between a gear merchant and a place that sold clockwork pets that were more alive than they should have been. Her sign read **ORRERY'S ODDMENTS — GOODS FROM EVERYWHERE** and the "everywhere" was underlined twice.

She was waiting for them. She had tea ready. This was not remarkable in itself — many shopkeepers prepared for customers — but the tea was from Harmonia, brewed in a pot from Maple Heights, and served in cups that didn't belong to any world any of them recognized.

"Sit," she said. "We don't have much time. You never do, in this phase."

She poured. The tea smelled like the Corrupted Forest. The cups felt like suburban ceramic. The table was definitely brass.

"You've done this before," she said. It was not a question. "Different world, same shape. Same villain at the top. Same speech about order and purpose and everything being where it belongs."

The party sat with that for a moment.

"The chancellor," one of them said.

"The coordinator," said another.

"The same person," said Madame Orrery, setting down the teapot with the finality of someone closing a book. "Or the same *thing*, wearing different costumes. Playing different roles. Running different... scenarios."

"How do you know this?"

"Because I sell goods from every world, dear. And every world has the same thumbprint on it. The same design philosophy. The same obsessive need for *balance.*" She said the word like it tasted sour. "Whoever built these places — all of them, not just this one — they're not trying to create worlds. They're trying to create *challenges.* Controlled environments where you can be tested, measured, and evaluated."

She looked at them over her teacup.

"The question isn't who they are. You'll find that out soon enough. The question is: what happens when they decide you've passed?"

### Chapter 2: The Name

The Grand Mechanism was not a building. It was a *statement.*

It occupied the center of Cogsworth Junction like a heart occupies a chest — essential, hidden, and responsible for everything that moved. From the outside, it looked like a cathedral made of gears. From the inside, it looked like the inside of a clock, if the clock were the size of a city and kept time for multiple realities.

The Warden of the Mainspring met them at the entrance. It was made of brass and iron and something else — something that hummed at a frequency that made their teeth ache. It fought like a vault door: immovable, impenetrable, and ultimately irrelevant once you found the combination.

The combination, it turned out, was persistence.

Inside, the Mechanism was alive. Not literally — or maybe literally, it was hard to tell in a world where clocks had opinions — but functionally alive. Gears shifted the layout as they walked. Corridors rearranged. Rooms rotated. The dungeon was short but disorienting, and every room felt less like a challenge and more like a *diagnostic.* As though the building itself was taking their measurements.

In a drafting room deep inside the Mechanism, they found the blueprints.

They were spread across a desk the size of a banquet table, held down by gear-shaped paperweights that ticked softly. The blueprints showed Cogsworth Junction. But they also showed other things. Overlaid, like transparencies stacked on a projector:

Harmonia. The same roads, the same distances, different names.

Maple Heights. The same proportions, the same population density, different aesthetic.

And two more they hadn't seen yet. One industrial. One made of light.

The same handwriting labeled all of them. Neat. Precise. Annotated with terms like "encounter density," "difficulty curve," "player retention zone."

At the bottom of the stack, a note: **If subjects reach the Mechanism ahead of schedule, accelerate reveal protocol. The masks are becoming transparent.**

The Tempo of the Mainspring found them in the gear room — the vast chamber where the primary mechanisms turned, each gear the size of a house, each rotation measured in decades. It was fast. Faster than the Tempo of the Hunt, faster than anything they'd faced. It fought like a second hand — relentless, circular, always coming back around.

They broke it apart. Its gears scattered across the floor and ticked to a stop, one by one, like a watch being disassembled.

The Regulator's chamber was at the heart of everything.

---

It was quiet. That was the first wrong thing. In a world defined by the sound of machinery, the Regulator's chamber was silent. Not peaceful-silent. *Controlled*-silent. The silence of a room where sound had been deliberately removed, like a variable set to zero.

The Regulator stood at a console. Not a throne, not an altar, not a dramatic perch. A *console.* With dials. And readouts. And a screen showing numbers that the party recognized, because they were *their* numbers.

Battles fought. Battles automated. Items used. Items hoarded. Deaths. Near-deaths. Time spent grinding. Time spent exploring. Time spent standing still, doing nothing, while the player behind the party decided what to do next.

"You cleared the medieval phase in efficient time," the Regulator said, not turning around. Their voice was measured. Calibrated. "Your automation rate was notable. Your resource management was—" a pause, a dial adjustment — "acceptable."

They turned. The Regulator looked like a Victorian engineer. Goggles pushed up on the forehead. Brass-buttoned coat. Gloves stained with oil that might have been oil and might have been something else. A face that was familiar in a way that couldn't be placed — not because they'd seen it before, but because they'd seen its *expression* before. On Mordaine. On the Coordinator. The same calm certainty. The same absolute belief in the system.

"The suburban phase was faster than projected," the Regulator continued. "You found the monitor. That was not scheduled until the third act. I adjusted, but the data was already compromised."

"You're them," a party member said. Not asked. Said. "The chancellor. The coordinator."

The Regulator tilted their head. The gesture was identical to one Mordaine had made in the throne room. Exactly identical.

"Those were roles," the Regulator said. "Appropriate to their context. A medieval world requires a medieval villain. A suburban world requires a suburban villain. This world required—" they gestured at themselves, at the console, at the gears — "an engineer."

"And the next world?"

The Regulator smiled. It was the same smile. Mordaine's smile. The Coordinator's smile. Practiced, patient, and utterly convinced of its own necessity.

"The next world won't need a costume. You've seen behind the curtain. There's no point in another mask."

They straightened. The dials on the console began to move on their own.

"My name," they said, "is not the Regulator. It is not the Chancellor. It is not the Coordinator. Those were... calibrations."

A pause. The gears of the Mechanism, silent until now, began to turn. Slowly. Building.

"I am the Calibrant. And you—"

They looked at the screen. At the numbers. At the profile of a party that had broken through two worlds and arrived at the third ahead of schedule, with strategies the system hadn't predicted and behaviors the models couldn't account for.

"—are out of tolerance."

The fight was unlike anything before. The Calibrant didn't fight like a villain. They fought like a *system administrator.* Mid-battle, the party watched their own stats change — defense lowered, attack buffed, speed redistributed. The Calibrant wasn't trying to beat them. They were trying to *balance* them. To find the exact configuration of difficulty that would make the fight fair.

The party won anyway. Not because they were stronger than the system. Because the system was trying to be fair, and they were not.

The Calibrant didn't dissolve this time. They didn't glitch. They didn't fade.

They simply stopped fighting. Lowered their hands. Looked at the party with something that might have been respect, if respect could be expressed in statistical terms.

"This mask is spent," they said. "The next one won't be so polite."

They walked backward into the console. Into the gears. Into the machinery itself. Not disappearing — *integrating.* Becoming the system they had always been part of.

And then the world began to disassemble.

Not break. Not collapse. *Disassemble.* Like a stage crew striking a set after the final performance. The gears slowed and separated. The brass walls folded inward. The sky — the penny-colored, gaslit sky — peeled back like wallpaper, revealing something underneath.

Gray. Concrete. Fluorescent lighting. The hum of industrial machinery without the romance of steam. A world stripped of beauty and pretense, built for function and nothing else.

The party stood in the ruins of Cogsworth Junction and watched the steampunk world pack itself away, piece by piece, like a clock being put back in its box.

"They're not even hiding it anymore," someone said.

No. They weren't.

The industrial world didn't welcome them. It simply *was.* No portal. No transition. No ceremony. One world ended and another began, and the seam between them was visible, deliberate, and utterly without apology.

The Calibrant was done performing. From here on, it was just the math.

*End of World 3*
