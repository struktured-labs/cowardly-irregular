# The Judge

## A Short Story — Arbiter Sub-Job Adoption

---

### Part 1: The Grade

The Arbiter of Steel said something before she fell that the Mage wrote down.

Not the speech. The speech was what you'd expect from an honorable duelist — respect, acknowledgment, the specific grace of someone who valued the exchange more than the outcome. The party heard the speech and nodded and moved on.

What the Mage wrote down was the thing she said *during* the fight. The thing nobody else caught because they were busy not dying.

"Your timing is faster than it looks."

Six words. Observational, not threatening. The Mage had been in the middle of a fire spell chain — three fireballs in sequence, timed to the gaps in her parry cycle — and the Arbiter had seen the timing, assessed it, classified it, and delivered a verdict. All in the space between one parry and the next.

She was *grading* him.

Not the way teachers grade — with rubrics and standards and the comfortable distance of someone who has already decided what competence looks like. She was grading him the way a mirror grades — by showing you exactly what you are, with no editorial between the image and the viewer.

After the fight, the Mage picked up her sword. Like the Fighter with the Warden's blade, nothing happened — no light, no sound, no cinematic announcement. The sword was a clean dueling weapon, well-balanced, with a single notch on the guard where someone had scored a hit that the Arbiter had never quite forgiven herself for. He could feel the notch's history in his fingers. He could feel her *disappointment* in it — not at the opponent who'd scored the hit, but at herself for permitting it.

"Why are you taking that?" the Cleric asked.

The Mage looked at the sword. At his staff. At the sword again.

"Because she was right," he said. "My timing *is* faster than it looks. She was the first person who ever measured it accurately."

He carried the sword. The party moved on.

And the Mage began to measure things.

---

### Part 2: Rubric

It started small. In the Corrupted Forest, tracking the Tempo of the Hunt, the Mage began annotating his field notes differently.

Previously his notes had been *analytical* — observations about magical theory, elemental interactions, the architectural patterns of the corruption spreading through the trees. Academic notes. Notes that asked *how does this work?*

Now the notes asked: *how well does this work?*

"The Cleric's healing efficiency is approximately 73% of theoretical maximum," he told the Fighter, in a camp by the river, while the Cleric was out of earshot.

The Fighter looked at him. "What?"

"She overheals. Consistently. She casts Cure when the target is at 60% health, but Cure restores 45% — which means 5% of every cast is wasted on health that was already there. If she waited until the target was at 55%, she'd achieve—"

"She's keeping us alive."

"She's keeping us alive *inefficiently.*"

The Fighter stared at him for a long moment. "Since when do you rate the Cleric?"

"I'm not *rating* her. I'm observing." The Mage adjusted his notes. "There's a difference between judgment and measurement."

"Is there?"

The Mage opened his mouth, found no answer, and wrote that down too. *Is there a difference between judgment and measurement? — Investigate.*

He investigated. He investigated by measuring everything.

The Rogue's approach angles: optimal 68% of the time, suboptimal 24%, actively counterproductive 8%. The Fighter's shield coverage: 91% of the party's left flank, 74% of the right, 12% gap at center-rear that the Cleric was unconsciously compensating for. The Bard's song selection: emotionally correct 95% of the time, tactically correct 61%, the delta explained by the Bard's preference for narrative satisfaction over combat efficiency.

He wrote all of this down. He showed none of it to the party.

But the party noticed anyway, because the Mage had started *muttering*.

"B plus," he said, under his breath, when the Rogue disarmed a trap in the forest dungeon. Not loudly. Not deliberately. A reflex — the word arriving in his mouth the way "good morning" arrives, automatic and unexamined.

The Rogue froze. "What did you say?"

"Nothing."

"You said B plus."

"I said — it was a good disarm. Technically proficient. The angle of approach could have been—"

"You *graded* me."

"I assessed. There's a—"

"If you say 'there's a difference,' I'm going to show you a difference between a disarmed trap and a rearmed one."

### Part 3: The Teacher

World 2 made it worse.

The Mage became the Science Nerd — a translation that fit uncomfortably well, because the Science Nerd's entire relationship with the world was through measurement, classification, and the specific confidence of someone who believes that understanding a thing is the same as having power over it.

The Arbiter sub-job settled into this new skin like a hand into a glove. The Science Nerd measured. Not just combat anymore — *everything.* Conversation quality. Decision speed. The efficiency of the party's route through the suburban sprawl. The rate at which the Cleric — the School Nurse now — consumed healing items versus the rate at which healing items could be acquired. He built spreadsheets. In his head. Running continuously.

"You're doing it again," the School Nurse said, in the parking lot of a strip mall where they'd stopped to regroup.

"Doing what?"

"That thing with your eyes. Where you look at someone and your lips move and I can *see* the grade forming before you speak."

"I don't grade people."

"You graded the hot dog vendor."

"The hot dog vendor's technique was *actively dangerous.* The meat-to-bun ratio was—"

"You graded. A hot dog vendor. On his hot dog technique."

The Science Nerd fell silent. The silence had the quality of someone hearing an echo of themselves for the first time and not liking the sound.

The Arbiter of the Grade found them at the intersection of academic rigor and municipal authority — a teacher who had been grading things for so long he no longer remembered what class he was teaching. Slacks, a clipboard, reading glasses pushed up on his forehead. He looked exactly like what the Science Nerd was becoming.

"This is an unannounced assessment," the Arbiter of the Grade announced. "Standard grading applies. Please show your work."

The Science Nerd felt a chill that had nothing to do with temperature.

"I grade on effort as well as outcome," the Arbiter continued. "That said, effort does not guarantee passing."

"He sounds like you," the Bard — the Band Kid — whispered.

"He does not sound like me."

"C minus on the denial," the Arbiter of the Grade said, from across the intersection. "Hesitation cost you the opening."

The fight was excruciating. Not physically — tactically. The Science Nerd fought the Arbiter and the Arbiter graded every action, and the Science Nerd *understood* every grade, and the understanding was worse than any damage because the grades were *accurate.* When the Arbiter said "B plus — very clean use of that ability," the Science Nerd could feel the assessment sliding into his own rubric, validating his measurement system, confirming that the world could be classified and that classification was power.

When the Arbiter said "Incomplete — you didn't finish the combo," the Science Nerd felt something beyond frustration. He felt *shame.* Not at the failure — at the incompleteness. At the asymmetry between what was attempted and what was achieved. The gap between intention and execution was, in the Arbiter's framework, the only thing worth measuring, and the Science Nerd had internalized that framework so thoroughly that the Arbiter's grades felt like his own.

They won. The Arbiter sat down, wrote "A — no conditions" on his clipboard, and said: "I don't know what class this is for. I've been grading it a long time. I'm not sure anymore what the subject is."

The Science Nerd stood over him and felt the vertigo of looking into a mirror that showed a future.

### Part 4: The Curve

"I need to talk to you," the Alchemist said.

World 3. Cogsworth Junction. The Mage had become the Alchemist, which was a translation that added precision instruments to the measurement problem and made everything quantifiably worse. The Alchemist had started rating things on a decimal scale — not just combat performance but *everything.* Meals. Sunsets. Conversations. The quality of the inn's mattress (6.2 — adequate support, poor temperature regulation, linen thread count below threshold for the price point).

The Cleric — the Steampriest — had found him in the workshop at 3 AM, surrounded by notes, building a rubric for *dreams.*

"What specifically is a good dream versus a bad dream," he had been muttering when she found him, "if we define 'good' as narrative coherence weighted against emotional regulation—"

"Mage."

He looked up. His eyes had the specific brightness of someone who has been measuring things for too long and has confused activity with progress.

"It's three in the morning," the Steampriest said.

"Is it?" He checked a clockwork chronometer. "3:14 AM. Fourteen minutes past the optimal REM cycle window. Which means the dream quality for this sleep period will be degraded by approximately—"

"*Mage.*"

He stopped. Not because she raised her voice — she hadn't. Because the specific quality of quiet in her voice was the one thing he hadn't built a rubric for.

"You are grading dreams," she said.

"I'm *classifying*—"

"You are building a rubric for unconscious experience. You are trying to assign a numerical value to what happens when people close their eyes. That is not science. That is not magic. That is a symptom."

The Alchemist set down his notes. The setting-down was deliberate — he could feel the Arbiter sub-job resisting it, the part of him that had been growing since the Arbiter of Steel's sword, the part that wanted to measure the act of setting down the notes and assign it a grade.

"When did it start?" the Steampriest asked, sitting beside him.

"In the cave. When she — the Arbiter of Steel — graded my timing."

"And what did it feel like?"

"It felt like being *seen.*" He said this quietly, and the quietness was not reluctance but precision — the precision of someone who is, for once, measuring himself accurately. "My whole life, I've been self-taught. Studying in a library with four books, two of which were menus. Nobody ever told me if I was doing it right. I figured things out by whether they worked, not by whether they were *good.* And then she — the Arbiter — she looked at my fire spell chain and she *knew.* She knew exactly how fast it was, exactly how well I'd timed it, exactly what grade it deserved. She saw my work. She assessed it. And the assessment was — it was the first time anyone had ever told me where I stood."

"And you wanted to feel that again."

"I wanted to *do* that again. To others. To everything. To measure the way she measured — cleanly, without ego, without cruelty. Just: this is what you are. Here is the grade. The grade is true."

"The grade is a frame," the Steampriest said. "And frames leave out everything that doesn't fit inside them."

### Part 5: The Assessment That Broke

The Arbiter of Efficiency in World 4 was the mirror the Alchemist had been dreading.

She was a quality inspector. A running display on her chest showed ratings in real-time: ACCEPTABLE, SUBOPTIMAL, EXCELLENT. She had a camera that logged everything. She had classified thousands of engagements. She had never encountered anything she couldn't classify.

Her personality was the Alchemist's personality accelerated to its logical endpoint: total measurement, total confidence, the specific condescension of someone who has never been wrong and has mistaken that record for proof that wrongness is impossible.

"Engagement parameters received," she announced. "Your combat performance will be classified and logged. Classification informs future Calibrant adjustments."

The Alchemist felt the sub-job *sing.* Not metaphorically — the Arbiter's sword at his hip vibrated with a frequency that matched the quality inspector's diagnostic displays. Two measurement systems recognizing each other. Two frames that classified the world the same way.

"Please perform at maximum capacity," the Arbiter of Efficiency said. Then, with the specific tone of someone delivering a verdict before the trial: "Though based on initial observation, maximum capacity may not be especially high."

The Alchemist felt his own lip curl. *Based on initial observation.* He knew that tone. He had used that tone. On the hot dog vendor. On the Cleric's healing efficiency. On the Rogue's approach angles. On dreams.

"We will see," the Arbiter said.

"Yes," the Alchemist said. "We will."

He fought her with everything he had. Fire spells timed to the millisecond. Ice chains exploiting the gaps in her diagnostic cycle. Elemental combinations he'd been theorizing about since World 1. He fought the way the Arbiter sub-job wanted him to fight — with measurement, with classification, with the absolute precision of someone who has a rubric for everything and believes in the rubric the way other people believe in gods.

And the Arbiter of Efficiency graded every move.

"Action: SUBOPTIMAL. That spell timing cost you the window."

He adjusted. Tighter timing. Better.

"Action: ADEQUATE — barely."

Adjusted again. Faster. Cleaner.

"Action: ANOMALOUS — reclassifying — reclassifying—"

The display started cycling. He had done something the rubric didn't cover — a triple-element fusion that shouldn't have been possible at his level, a technique born not from measurement but from the self-taught improvisation that had been his method before any Arbiter told him what a grade was.

"CLASSIFICATION PENDING," the display read, and kept reading it, and the Arbiter of Efficiency fought on with a spinning indicator where her certainty used to be.

The Alchemist watched the display cycle and felt something crack.

Not in the Arbiter. In himself.

The display was his display. The spinning was his spinning. Every time he'd encountered something that didn't fit his rubric — the Bard's song choices that were emotionally perfect and tactically wrong, the Rogue's approach angles that worked despite being "suboptimal," the Cleric's overhealing that wasted mana but kept the party's morale intact — his own internal display had cycled. CLASSIFICATION PENDING. And he had forced a grade anyway, because the alternative — admitting that some things can't be measured — was a system state he'd been told was impossible.

The Arbiter of Efficiency went down. Her display stopped on UNCLASSIFIABLE.

"This has not happened before," she said. "In all my engagements, this has not happened before."

The Alchemist knelt beside her. Not to observe. Not to classify. To witness — which was a different act, one that the rubric had no column for.

"I don't know what to do with unclassifiable," the Arbiter said. The condescension was gone from her voice. In its place was the specific quality of a worldview encountering its first counterevidence. "I assumed my classification system was complete."

"So did I," the Alchemist said.

"I assumed my classification system was complete," she repeated, as though the sentence needed to be heard twice to be believed.

"So did I," the Alchemist repeated, and meant it both times.

### Part 6: What the Judge Learns

The party was around a fire in the industrial barracks when the Alchemist said: "I owe you all an apology."

The Rogue looked up. "For the B plus?"

"For the B plus. For the C minus I gave your lockpicking in World 3 that I thought you didn't hear. For the 73% healing efficiency I calculated for the Cleric. For the 61% tactical correctness I assigned to the Bard's songs. For the 6.2 I gave the mattress."

"That mattress was a 4 at best," the Bard said.

"The mattress is not the point." The Alchemist held up the Arbiter's sword — the dueling blade with the notch on the guard. "This sword taught me to measure. Measurement is useful. Measurement is *necessary* — I'm a mage, my entire discipline is quantified understanding of natural forces. But measurement as a *personality* is something else. Measurement as the only way you see the world — that's not understanding. That's a cage with numbers on the bars."

"The Calibrant measures everything," the Fighter said quietly. "The Wardens test defense. The Arbiters test offense. The Tempos test speed. The Curators test resources. It's all measurement. The whole system is a rubric."

"And I was running the rubric from inside the party." The Alchemist set the sword down. Not dropped — placed, the way you place something you're choosing to stop carrying. "The Arbiter of Steel told me my timing was faster than it looked, and that was the first time anyone had measured me accurately, and I loved it so much I tried to do it to everyone else. But measurement without mercy is just — surveillance. It's the Calibrant's job. Not mine."

"Can you stop?" the Steampriest asked.

The Alchemist picked the sword back up. Because he wasn't done with it — not the way the Fighter wasn't done with the Warden's blade, not because the sub-job wouldn't let go, but because the measurement was still *useful.* The Arbiter's precision, the ability to see clearly, to assess accurately, to know exactly where you stood — that was a gift. The gift had just been wearing the wrong clothes.

"I can't stop measuring," he said. "But I can stop grading. Measurement tells you what *is.* Grading tells you what it's *worth.* The first one is magic. The second one is — the second one is what the Calibrant does."

"There's a difference between a mirror and a judge," the Bard said.

"Exactly."

"You'd have given that line a B plus before."

"It's an A," the Alchemist said, and smiled, and the smile was the first thing in three worlds that he hadn't measured before he let it happen.

---

### Epilogue: The Ungraded

The Alchemist still measured things. He couldn't stop — the sub-job was part of him, the way the Warden's sword was part of the Fighter, the way every Masterite's gift was part of whoever carried it. The Arbiter's precision ran beneath his magic like a subtext beneath a sentence, informing everything without controlling it.

But the grades stopped.

In their place was something harder and more honest: observation without verdict. The Cleric's healing was 73% efficient. That was a fact. The fact didn't need a grade because efficiency wasn't the right frame for what healing was. Healing was presence. Healing was the decision to keep someone standing when the numbers said they should fall. The Cleric's 27% "waste" was not waste — it was margin. It was the space between surviving and being *okay,* and okay was not a measurable state.

The Rogue's approach angles were suboptimal 24% of the time. That was also a fact. But the Rogue's suboptimal angles worked because the Rogue understood something the rubric didn't — that suboptimal for *this* fight might be optimal for the *next* fight, that positioning was not a single calculation but a conversation with the future, and conversations couldn't be graded because they weren't finished yet.

The Bard's songs were tactically correct 61% of the time. And the other 39% were *beautiful,* and beauty was UNCLASSIFIABLE, and UNCLASSIFIABLE was the honest answer, and honest answers were better than forced grades, even when they left a blank field in the form.

The Alchemist walked through World 5 and World 6 with the Arbiter's sword at his hip and the Arbiter's precision in his eyes and a blank field where the grades used to go. The blank field was not a failure of measurement. It was the measurement working correctly for the first time — measuring its own limits, classifying itself as incomplete, and finding that the incompleteness was not a bug.

It was the system working as designed.

Not the Calibrant's design. His.

*End of The Judge*
