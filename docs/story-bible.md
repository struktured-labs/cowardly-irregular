# Cowardly Irregular — Story Bible

## Two-Layer Narrative Structure

Each overworld tells a self-contained genre story (Layer 1) while the meta-narrative (Layer 2) bleeds through progressively. Players don't know the meta is happening at first.

| World | Genre | Surface Story | Meta Bleed | Calibrant's Approach |
|-------|-------|--------------|------------|---------------------|
| Medieval | Arthurian fantasy | The Usurper's Crown | ~5% — dismissible NPC humor | Hidden, performing as fantasy villain |
| Suburban | EarthBound weird Americana | The Neighborhood Problem | ~20% — characters question transitions | Hidden, performing as bureaucratic villain |
| Steampunk | Victorian thriller (SHORT) | The Regulator | ~50% — The Calibrant is named | Exposed but still performing — elegant control |
| Industrial | Brutalist corporate dystopia | TBD | ~75% — systems visibly breaking | Gloves off — raw efficiency, no more art |
| Digital | Cyber/glitch | TBD | ~90% — fourth wall is gone | Direct manipulation — the game IS the world |
| Abstract | Existential minimalism | TBD | 100% — you ARE the meta | Unmasked — nothing left to hide behind |

## Character Identity

### Core Principle: ALL party members have full personalities, not just the lead.

Party members have no *fixed* personalities — but they always HAVE personalities. Identity emerges from a layered system where every input shifts how each character speaks, acts, and relates to others:

#### Layer 1: Base Job (foundation)
The starting disposition. A Fighter is direct. A Cleric is attentive. A Mage is analytical. A Rogue is perceptive. A Bard is expressive. These aren't stereotypes — they're starting vectors that everything else modifies.

#### Layer 2: World Evolution (automatic drift)
Jobs auto-evolve per world (see `job_evolutions_by_world.md`). Each evolution shifts personality contextually:
- Fighter → Security Guard: directness becomes procedural ("Sir, I'm going to have to ask you to leave")
- Fighter → Steamknight: directness becomes mechanical precision
- Fighter → Enforcer: directness becomes institutional weight
- Fighter → Firewall: directness becomes logical rules
- Fighter → Resolve: directness becomes the pure concept of standing firm

#### Layer 3: Secondary Job (personality accent)
The secondary job creates tension/harmony with the primary:
- Fighter + Cleric = stoic protector (duty + faith)
- Fighter + Speculator = mercenary (duty + profit)
- Fighter + Rogue = pragmatic survivor (duty + opportunism)
- Mage + Bard = theatrical intellectual (analysis + performance)
- Cleric + Rogue = conflicted moralist (faith + edge)

#### Layer 4: Tertiary Job (if applicable — subtle flavor)
A third job adds a barely-perceptible undercurrent. A Fighter/Cleric/Bard has a hint of showmanship in their stoic protection. Subtle but detectable in key dialogue moments.

#### Layer 5: Playstyle (behavioral overlay — THE BIG ONE)
This is where the real drift happens. The game tracks how each character is USED, not just what they are:

| Playstyle | Personality Drift | Speech Pattern |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| Traditional manual | Disciplined, present, engaged | Direct statements, specific observations |
| Heavy autobattle | Detached, strategic, speaks in abstractions | "The system handled it." / "Optimal." |
| One-shot builds | Arrogant, precise, impatient with inefficiency | "That should have been faster." |
| Grind-heavy | Patient, meditative, speaks in rhythms | "Again." / "One more." / repetitive calm |
| Autogrind | Zen-like detachment, philosophical distance | "It runs. We grow. That's enough." |
| Exploit-heavy | Sly, meta-aware, breaks fourth wall earliest | "That shouldn't work. It works." |
| Conservative/defensive | Cautious, risk-averse, speaks in contingencies | "What if we..." / "Just in case." |
| Risky/aggressive | Bold, impatient, dismissive of caution | "We'll figure it out." / "Go." |
| Dialogue-skipping | Terse, action-oriented, borderline rude | "..." / "Can we go?" / "Skip." |

**These compound.** A grinding autobattler speaks differently than a grinding manual player. An exploit-heavy one-shotter is cocky AND sly. A conservative dialogue-skipper is anxious but impatient about it.

### Party Dynamics

**Player 1** reflects the player most directly — their playstyle adaptation is strongest.

**Party members 2-4** each have their OWN personality trajectory based on THEIR job combinations and how THEY are used. If the player uses character 2 as the healer who never attacks, character 2 develops a caretaker personality. If character 3 is always on autobattle, character 3 becomes detached and observational.

**Inter-party friction** emerges naturally: a manually-played Fighter clashes with an autobattled Mage. A grinding Cleric has patience a one-shot Rogue doesn't. These tensions show up in rest-point banter and key story moments.

**The novellas show ONE possible personality configuration** — the "canonical playthrough." The actual game generates personality dynamically.

## World 1 — Medieval: "The Usurper's Crown"

### Setting

The kingdom centered around Harmonia was once peaceful and unremarkable. The king was kind but weak. His chancellor, **Mordaine**, accumulated power quietly — whispering policy, replacing advisors, tightening control — until the king became a puppet and the kingdom a police state dressed in pageantry.

### Tone

Classic fantasy played mostly straight. Earnest but not humorless. The party isn't chosen — they're conscripts, drifters, and misfits who got swept up. Nobody expected them to matter.

### Key Characters

**Chancellor Mordaine**
- The antagonist's mask in the medieval world — the player doesn't know this yet
- Sorceress and political mastermind — Morgan le Fay meets Jafar
- Seized power through manipulation, not conquest
- Genuinely believes order requires control
- Not cartoonishly evil — she thinks the kingdom is better under her rule
- Final dialogue should feel almost reasonable, making the player uncomfortable
- In hindsight (after World 3+ reveals), her speech patterns and philosophy match every other overworld villain exactly

**King Aldric**
- The puppet king, once beloved
- Still technically on the throne — Mordaine rules through him
- Whether he can be saved or is too far gone is a late-act question
- Tragic figure, not a villain

**The Irregulars (the party)**
- Not heroes. Not chosen. Just people who were in the wrong place.
- Drafted, volunteered out of boredom, or stumbled into it
- Their reputation as "irregulars" is an insult from the kingdom's real military
- They succeed because nobody takes them seriously

### Quest Progression

#### Act 1: "Nobody Special"
**Location**: Harmonia Village → Overworld → Whispering Cave

The party starts in Harmonia. Something is wrong — monster activity is up, trade routes are cut, the cave to the north has gotten dangerous. The village elder asks for help because nobody else will come. The real army is busy "protecting the capital" (read: enforcing Mordaine's rule).

**Beats:**
1. Talk to Elder Theron — learn about the cave, get equipped
2. Scholar Milo introduces autobattle as a practical tool ("why fight manually when you can think strategically?")
3. Explore the village, meet NPCs — Phil the Lost hints at something weird but it's easy to ignore
4. Head to the overworld, fight basic encounters (slimes, bats, goblins)
5. Enter Whispering Cave — first real dungeon
6. Boss: **Warden of the Old Guard** (Masterite, Phase 1) — a guardian entity that tests whether you deserve to proceed
7. After beating the Warden, find evidence that the cave's corruption is being fed deliberately — traces of court magic

**Meta bleed:** Phil the Lost's NPC-awareness dialogue. The "???" NPC knowing about save files. Easy to dismiss as comedy.

#### Act 2: "The Forest Knows"
**Location**: Corrupted Forest → Eldertree Village

The party follows the trail of court magic into the Corrupted Forest. The corruption here is more aggressive — the forest itself resists intruders. Eldertree Village is caught in the middle, their sacred trees dying.

**Beats:**
1. Eldertree villagers describe the corruption spreading from the capital's direction
2. The forest has been warped — encounters are stranger, environment shifts
3. Meet an NPC who fled the capital and knows Mordaine's name — first concrete lead
4. Deep in the forest, confront the **Arbiter of Steel** (Masterite) — an offensive powerhouse that seems to be *guarding* something
5. Behind the Arbiter: a sealed passage toward the capital, and a message — "The crown is not what it seems"

**Meta bleed:** One NPC in Eldertree says something like "I've seen you before. In another... version of this." Played as delirium from the corruption. An item description references "data" instead of "magic."

#### Act 3: "The Capital"
**Location**: Scriptura (capital city)

The party reaches Scriptura. It's prosperous on the surface — Mordaine keeps things running efficiently. But dissent is crushed, information is controlled, and the Scriptweaver's Guild has been co-opted.

**Beats:**
1. Enter the city in disguise or through back channels
2. Gather intel from resistance contacts — learn the king is under enchantment
3. Mordaine is aware of the party but considers them beneath notice
4. Side encounters with city guards, propaganda, and citizens who genuinely support Mordaine (moral ambiguity)
5. **Tempo of the Hunt** (Masterite) appears as Mordaine's enforcer — fast and relentless, hunts the party through the city streets
6. Defeat Tempo, gain access to the palace district
7. Optional: visit the Scriptweaver's Guild for lore and potentially unlock advanced job hints (if debug mode / progression allows)

**Meta bleed:** The Scriptweaver's Guild has books that describe "functions" and "variables." A scholar mentions "the one who designed this place." Still deniable as metaphor.

#### Act 4: "The Throne"
**Location**: The Royal Palace

Confrontation with Mordaine. The palace is a dungeon — trapped, guarded, politically charged.

**Beats:**
1. Navigate the palace — encounters with elite guards, magical traps
2. Find King Aldric — he's aware but powerless, begs for help or warns you away (depending on party composition/playstyle)
3. **Curator of the Flame** (Masterite) guards the throne room — drains resources, tests endurance
4. Confront Mordaine in the throne room
5. Boss fight: Chancellor Mordaine — uses court magic, summons guards, manipulates the battlefield
6. Mordaine is defeated but doesn't die in the typical way — she dissolves, glitches, or simply stops. Her final words are genre-appropriate but carry a double meaning: something like "This kingdom needed order. You needed a challenge. Both served their purpose."
7. The world shifts. The portal to the suburban world opens. It feels less like a reward and more like... the next level was loaded.

**Meta bleed:** Mordaine's defeat feels wrong — not triumphant, just... complete. Like a phase ending. The player won't realize until later that Mordaine was never a person, just a role the antagonist was playing. First-time players will just think it was a weird ending. Replayers will catch the shared vocabulary with later villains.

## World 2 — Suburban: "The Neighborhood Problem" (Outline)

### Setting
Maple Heights — a 90s American suburb. Eerily normal. Something is wrong beneath the surface. Kids going missing, the strip mall rearranges overnight, the HOA has enforcement powers that feel supernatural.

### Tone
EarthBound. Mundane objects are threatening. Adults are oblivious or complicit. The weird is treated as normal by locals.

### Surface Villain
**The Coordinator** — HOA president / PTA chair hybrid. Bureaucratic tyranny. Controls the neighborhood through rules, committees, and an inexplicable ability to make people comply.

### Key Differences from World 1
- Party still has their medieval equipment — NPCs comment on it
- Magic works but people call it other things ("that's just static electricity")
- The party's job-based personalities clash with suburban normalcy
- A Fighter walking around with a sword gets the cops called
- Autobattle feels different here — the enemies are weird (sentient lawn mowers, aggressive mailboxes, HOA drones)

### Meta Bleed (~20%)
- NPCs reference "the place you came from" directly
- Someone asks "why does your save file have medieval timestamps?"
- A store sells items from World 1 alongside suburban equivalents
- The Coordinator's office has a monitor showing World 1 stats

## World 3 — Steampunk: "The Regulator"

### Setting

Cogsworth Junction and Brasston — a clockwork civilization that runs on precision. Every citizen is on schedule. Every gear has a purpose. The trains run on time because deviation is not tolerated. It's beautiful, efficient, and suffocating.

### Tone

Victorian thriller. Shorter than Worlds 1 and 2 — this is the pivot, not a full adventure. The surface story is compressed because the *real* story is what's happening underneath: the player figuring out that something connects all of this.

### The Calibrant's Mask: The Regulator

**The Regulator** — chief engineer of the Grand Mechanism, the clockwork system that governs all of Cogsworth Junction. Speaks in terms of efficiency, tolerances, and acceptable margins. Controls the city through literal machinery — every building, every schedule, every citizen's daily routine is mechanized.

- Presents as a Victorian industrialist obsessed with order
- But their language is *too* familiar: "acceptable parameters," "deviation must be corrected," "the system requires balance"
- A party member (or an NPC) finally says it: "That's what the chancellor said. That's what the coordinator said. Word for word."
- The Regulator doesn't deny it. Just pauses. Smiles. "You're ahead of schedule."

### Quest Progression

This world has 2 acts, not 4. It's tight and revelatory.

#### Act 1: "Clockwork"
**Location**: Brasston Village → Cogsworth Junction overworld

The party arrives and immediately feels the control. Everything in Brasston runs like clockwork — literally. Citizens move on rails, shops open and close on the second, and deviation triggers mechanical enforcers.

**Beats:**
1. Arrive in Brasston — existing NPCs react to outsiders. Sprocket welcomes you but warns about "the schedule." Clem the Lamplighter hints at something living in the pipes.
2. The party notices the enforcers — clockwork sentinels that correct "irregularities." The party IS an irregularity.
3. Madame Orrery (the cross-world merchant) is the first to say it plainly: "You've done this before. Different world, same shape. Same villain at the top."
4. Brigadier Flux, the retired engineer, drops the key piece: "The Regulator arrived the same day the old chief engineer vanished. Walked in like they'd always been here. Knew every pipe, every gear. As if they'd *designed* it."
5. The party pushes into Cogsworth Junction proper — the mechanical overworld. Encounters are clockwork enemies, automated defenses.
6. Masterite encounter: **Warden of the Mainspring** — mechanical tank boss guarding the central works

#### Act 2: "The Name"
**Location**: The Grand Mechanism (dungeon) → The Regulator's Chamber

The Grand Mechanism is the heart of Cogsworth Junction — a massive clockwork engine that literally runs the world. Inside, the party finds something wrong: the gears don't just run the city. They run *the transitions between worlds.*

**Beats:**
1. Inside the Mechanism — the dungeon is short but disorienting. Gears shift the layout. The environment feels *designed* to test you, not just block you.
2. Find records/blueprints that show the Mechanism connecting to other worlds — medieval, suburban. The same handwriting on all of them.
3. Masterite encounter: **Tempo of the Mainspring** — speed-based boss that manipulates the clockwork environment
4. Reach the Regulator's chamber at the heart of the Mechanism
5. Confrontation with The Regulator:
   - The Regulator doesn't monologue about their steampunk plans. Instead, they talk about *you.*
   - "You cleared the medieval phase in [X] turns. You automated [Y]% of your battles. You're running [ahead of / behind / exactly on] schedule."
   - They have stats. Your stats. They've been watching since World 1.
   - A party member connects it: "Mordaine. The Coordinator. You."
   - The Regulator: "Those were... calibrations. This world was supposed to be where you stopped asking questions. Clearly, I miscalculated."
   - The Regulator names themselves for the first time: **"I am The Calibrant. And you are out of tolerance."**
6. Boss fight: The Regulator / The Calibrant — uses mechanical attacks, summons clockwork minions, and for the first time, *adjusts difficulty mid-fight* (visible stat changes, healing, phase shifts)
7. Defeat: The Calibrant doesn't dissolve or glitch like before. They simply... leave. "This mask is spent. The next one won't be so polite." The clockwork world begins to wind down.

**Meta bleed (50%):**
- The Calibrant directly references your play statistics
- The world-connecting blueprints are undeniable
- An NPC says "the word you're looking for is 'game'"
- The transition to World 4 isn't a portal — the steampunk world literally *disassembles* around you, revealing the industrial world underneath, like scenery being struck from a stage

### Why This World Is Short

The steampunk world isn't a full adventure because its purpose is revelation, not exploration. The player has had two full genre stories. Now the rug gets pulled. The brevity IS the point — you barely settle in before everything unravels. The Calibrant doesn't give you time to get comfortable because they're done pretending.

## The Calibrant (Meta Villain — Full Game)

**The Calibrant** — the one who calibrates. One entity, many masks.

The antagonist is the singular force behind every overworld's "final boss." They are not multiple villains — they are one entity wearing genre-appropriate masks to regulate the player's experience. They believe they are doing their job: keeping the game fair, balanced, and challenging.

### The Masks

Each overworld's surface villain IS the antagonist, roleplaying as the final boss of that genre:

| World | Mask | Role in Genre |
|-------|------|--------------|
| Medieval | Chancellor Mordaine | Sorceress-usurper, classic fantasy villain |
| Suburban | The Coordinator | HOA president, bureaucratic tyrant |
| Steampunk | The Regulator | Chief engineer, clockwork tyrant — THE REVEAL |
| Industrial | TBD | Corporate overlord |
| Digital | TBD | Rogue AI / sysadmin |
| Abstract | Unmasked | The antagonist as themselves |

The player doesn't realize these are the same entity until the meta bleed makes it undeniable (World 3+). In retrospect, the clues were there — similar speech patterns, the same philosophy of "order through control," Masterites deployed the same way.

### Philosophy

- Sees themselves as a game designer / dungeon master
- Creates challenge environments (the overworlds) to test and regulate the player
- Deploys Masterites to profile player strengths before each confrontation
- Genuinely believes balance is necessary — unchecked player power would "break" everything
- Not evil — just convinced that the system matters more than the player's freedom

### Reveal Arc

- **World 1-2**: No awareness of the antagonist. Mordaine and The Coordinator feel like separate villains. Only subtle traces — system glitches after victories, NPC dialogue that's slightly too aware, Masterites that feel oddly systematic.
- **World 3**: The antagonist is named. Someone connects the dots — "the chancellor, the coordinator... they spoke the same way."
- **World 4**: The antagonist communicates directly. Drops the pretense of being a genre villain.
- **World 5**: Full confrontation. The antagonist is managing the game in real-time, visibly.
- **World 6 (Abstract)**: Unmasked. Final battle. The antagonist admits they can't keep up. Resolution varies by playstyle (see design-canon.md).

### Post-Defeat Dialogue (varies by playstyle)

The antagonist's final words adapt to how you played:
- **Autobattle-heavy**: "You didn't even fight me yourself. And somehow... that's worse."
- **Manual/traditional**: "You played by every rule I set. And still won. I have nothing left."
- **Exploit-heavy**: "You broke every system I built. I should be angry. I'm impressed."
- **Grind-heavy**: "You simply... outlasted me. No cleverness. No tricks. Just persistence. How do I counter that?"

## Dialogue Branching by Job/Playstyle

### How It Works

Key story moments have dialogue variants keyed to:

```
{
  "scene_id": "mordaine_confrontation",
  "variants": {
    "job:fighter": "You face Mordaine head-on. 'Another sword. How original.'",
    "job:mage": "Mordaine smirks. 'A mage challenges a mage. At least this will be interesting.'",
    "job:cleric": "Mordaine pauses. 'Faith? Here? How... quaint.'",
    "job:rogue": "'You snuck in,' Mordaine notes. 'I respect the effort, if not the odds.'",
    "style:autobattle": "Your party moves in mechanical precision. Mordaine frowns. 'You're not even HERE, are you?'",
    "style:manual": "Mordaine watches you carefully. 'Ah. A traditionalist. This will be personal.'",
    "default": "Mordaine regards you coldly. 'So. The irregulars.'"
  }
}
```

Not every line branches — just pivotal moments:
- First meeting with major NPCs
- Boss pre-fight dialogue
- Post-victory reactions
- World transition moments
- Party banter at rest points
